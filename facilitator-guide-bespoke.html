Section 1: Facilitator Preparation Guide

Experience Overview and Objectives

A Bespoke Experience is a fully tailored team experience built from the ground up around a client's specific theme, values, and objectives. There is no fixed format. The design is driven by discovery — understanding who the client is, what they need, and what outcome will make the experience genuinely meaningful. A Bespoke Experience may borrow elements from other Team Connect formats, combine multiple approaches, or be entirely original. The facilitator's role is both designer and guide: you must understand the client's world deeply enough to deliver something that feels made for them — because it was.

Primary objectives (customised per client — these are common examples):

  • Reinforce specific company values through experiential learning
  • Address a named team dynamic or cultural challenge
  • Celebrate a milestone, transition, or achievement with purpose
  • Build connection around a shared theme, narrative, or brand
  • Support a leadership team through a change process
  • Deliver a memorable, differentiated experience that reflects the client's identity

Ideal Group Size and Duration

  • Minimum: No fixed minimum — bespoke designs scale to the group
  • Maximum: No fixed maximum — design determines feasible participant range
  • Typical range: 10–500 participants depending on format
  • Duration: Determined during design phase — typically 1.5–4 hours
  • Team structure: Determined during design phase

The Discovery Process

The discovery process is the foundation of every Bespoke Experience. Without it, the design cannot serve the client's actual needs.

Discovery meeting agenda (60–90 minutes with client):

  1. Context: What is the occasion? What has the team been through recently? What is coming up?
  2. Objectives: What does success look like? What should participants feel, know, or be able to do differently after this experience?
  3. Values and themes: Are there specific company values, strategic themes, or cultural elements to weave in?
  4. Constraints: Budget, venue, time, accessibility, cultural sensitivities, participant preferences.
  5. Past experiences: What has this team done before? What landed well? What flopped?
  6. Sponsor goals: What does the person commissioning this experience personally need from it?

Discovery outputs:

  • A written brief summarising objectives, constraints, and success criteria
  • Agreement on the experience format and structure
  • Sign-off on timeline for design, review, and delivery

The Design Process

After discovery, the design phase produces the experience blueprint.

Design steps:

  1. Draft experience concept — format, flow, timing, themes.
  2. Share concept with client for feedback (allow 5 working days for revisions).
  3. Develop full facilitator script, challenge or activity specifications, and materials list.
  4. Finalise logistics: venue, equipment, external suppliers if needed.
  5. Internal review — run concept past a senior facilitator before finalising.
  6. Final client sign-off — minimum 1 week before delivery.

Design principles:

  • Every element should connect back to the client's stated objectives.
  • Avoid designing for complexity — elegant simplicity creates better experiences than elaborate productions.
  • The facilitator should be able to explain the design rationale for every choice.
  • Build in contingency — what happens if this element falls flat or runs long?

Facilitation Principles for Unknown Formats

Because every Bespoke Experience is different, facilitators must rely on core principles rather than fixed scripts.

  • Read the room continuously. Energy, confusion, excitement, and disengagement are real-time feedback. Adjust pacing, tone, and sequence in response.
  • Hold the container. Participants need to know they are in capable hands even when the format is unfamiliar. Confident, calm facilitation creates safety.
  • Connect moments to meaning. Every activity, every transition, every challenge needs to be linked to the experience's purpose — make that link explicit.
  • Manage the unexpected. Bespoke experiences have more unknowns than standard formats. Prepare for the most likely failure modes; improvise the rest.
  • Close every loop. Every activity should have a beginning, middle, and end. Incomplete experiences leave participants unsatisfied.

Debrief Approach for Bespoke Experiences

Because the format varies, the debrief must also be tailored. All bespoke debriefs share the same purpose: to make the experience's meaning explicit and actionable.

Debrief design questions (answered during preparation):

  • What is the one insight we most want participants to leave with?
  • What questions will reliably surface that insight?
  • How long does this group need to reflect? (Scale to group size and energy level)
  • What format serves this group best? (Open discussion, small groups, written reflection, visual output)

Client Communication Throughout Design Phase

  • After discovery meeting: send written brief within 3 working days for client confirmation.
  • After concept is drafted: share concept document and request feedback within 5 working days.
  • After client feedback: revise and share updated design for final sign-off.
  • One week before event: confirm all logistics, materials, and facilitator assignments.
  • 48 hours before: confirm all materials are ready and all external suppliers are confirmed.
  • Day before: confirm venue access, final headcount, and any last-minute changes.

Equipment and Materials Checklist

Developed during the design phase. Standard facilitator kit always includes:

  • Wireless microphone and PA system
  • Laptop and HDMI adapter
  • Printed backup materials (even if the experience is primarily digital)
  • Timer (visible to participants if relevant to format)
  • Markers, paper, and sticky notes (general creative facilitation tools)
  • Emergency contact numbers: client sponsor, venue coordinator, any external suppliers

Facilitator Briefing Notes

  • Know the client's brief and design rationale deeply before you enter the room.
  • Be able to explain the purpose of every element to any participant who asks.
  • Keep the client sponsor informed of any real-time changes you make during delivery.
  • Do not improvise the core structure without flagging it — note changes for debrief and future reference.
  • After the event, submit a delivery debrief note: what worked, what didn't, what you would change.

Pre-Event Client Communication Checklist

  • Discovery meeting completed and written brief confirmed by client
  • Concept document shared and approved
  • Final design document signed off (minimum 1 week before)
  • All logistics confirmed: venue, materials, suppliers, headcount
  • Participant information shared with client for distribution (Section 5)
  • Facilitator(s) fully briefed on design rationale and contingency plans
  • Final headcount confirmed (minimum 5 days before)

Section 2: Hosting Guide

Welcome and Opening Script (Adaptable Framework)

The welcome script for a Bespoke Experience must be written specifically for each event. It should always include:

1. Connection to the moment:

"You're here today because [CONTEXT: milestone / transition / challenge / celebration]. That matters — and so does the way we spend the next [DURATION] together."

2. Experience framing:

"What we're about to do was designed specifically for [COMPANY/TEAM NAME]. [BRIEFLY: what the experience is and why it was designed this way]."

3. Outcome intention:

"By the end of today, we want you to leave with [ONE CLEAR OUTCOME — e.g., a renewed sense of what we stand for / a specific commitment to your team / a shared memory that connects us]."

4. Permission and safety:

"This is a space to be fully present. Whatever is on your to-do list — let it wait. [COMPANY NAME] has given you this time. Use it."

Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

  • Open with connection, not instruction. The first five minutes set the tone — make them human, not administrative.
  • Monitor the room, not the clock. Adjust pacing to energy levels — rushing a reflective moment costs more than it saves.
  • Use music deliberately. Sound design can shift energy in a room quickly. Prepare playlists matched to each segment's intended energy.
  • Build in peaks and troughs. High-energy activities followed by reflective moments create a satisfying arc.
  • Close with intention. The final moment should be the most meaningful. Don't let the experience trail off — design a clear, powerful ending.

How to Handle Reluctant Participants

  • Meet reluctance with genuine curiosity: "What would make this worth your time today?"
  • Connect the experience's objectives to their actual work concerns.
  • Give them a meaningful role that serves their strengths.
  • Acknowledge when an experience is stretching — and name it as intentional.

How to Manage Dominant Participants

  • In bespoke experiences, dominant participants can derail carefully designed moments.
  • Redirect by structure: build roles and formats that distribute contribution.
  • If one person is shaping the group's output, explicitly invite other voices: "Before we move on, I want to hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet."

Common Problems and How to Solve Them

Activity falls flat / group disengagedPause and name it: "I'm noticing the energy has dipped — let's take a moment." Reset with a quick energiser before continuing.

Section 3: Debrief Guide

Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

A Bespoke Experience has been designed to serve a specific purpose for a specific group. The debrief is the moment where that purpose becomes explicit. Without it, even the most beautifully designed experience can feel like entertainment — memorable but shallow. With it, participants leave with insight that belongs to them and their context.

Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

  • Timing determined during design phase.
  • Minimum 20 minutes for any meaningful debrief.
  • Longer experiences warrant longer debriefs — 30–40 minutes for half-day formats.
  • The debrief should represent at least 20% of the total experience duration.

10 Debrief Questions (Adaptable Framework)

  1. "What happened just now? Walk me through what you noticed."
  2. "What surprised you most — about the experience, or about yourself, or about your team?"
  3. "At what moment did you feel most connected to the people around you? What made that happen?"
  4. "Where did you feel resistance or discomfort? What was underneath that?"
  5. "What did your team do well under pressure / uncertainty / creativity [adapt to experience]?"
  6. "What was harder than you expected? Why?"
  7. "What have you learned about yourself as a team member today?"
  8. "What have you learned about your team that you didn't know, or didn't name, before today?"
  9. "How does what you experienced here show up in your actual work — the real meetings, the real decisions, the real pressure?"
  10. "If you had to name one thing you want to do differently after today — what would it be?"

How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

Because the design is tailored, the connections are also tailored. During design, identify:

  • The two or three workplace behaviours the experience is intended to surface or shift.
  • The specific metaphors from the experience that connect to those behaviours.
  • The language the client uses to describe the challenge they are trying to address — use it in the debrief.

What to Leave the Group With

Determined during design phase. Options include:

  • A written commitment card
  • A group artefact created during the experience
  • A named team agreement
  • A shared metaphor or story to carry forward
  • A printed insight summary to take back to their desks

Section 4: Preparation Checklist

2 Weeks Before

  • Final design document signed off by client
  • All materials designed, printed, or ordered
  • All external suppliers confirmed
  • Venue confirmed with access time and AV requirements
  • Facilitator(s) assigned and briefed
  • Participant information sent to client for distribution (Section 5)
  • Contingency plans developed for two most likely failure modes
  • Final headcount confirmed

1 Week Before

  • All materials received and checked
  • Facilitator run sheet finalised with minute-by-minute timing
  • All technology tested (AV, digital platforms, streaming if relevant)
  • External suppliers confirmed with arrival/delivery times
  • Final headcount confirmed — adjust any materials accordingly
  • Internal facilitator briefing completed
  • Client sponsor briefed on experience flow and debrief approach

Day Before

  • Confirm venue access time
  • Pack all facilitator kit and experience materials
  • Charge all devices
  • Confirm external supplier delivery / arrival
  • Confirm final headcount and any accessibility requirements

Day of Event

  • Arrive at venue with full setup time (minimum 60–90 minutes before participants)
  • Set up experience space per design specifications
  • Test all technology
  • Brief any co-facilitators or assistants
  • Welcome client sponsor — confirm any last-minute changes
  • Welcome participants
  • Deliver experience
  • Submit post-event delivery debrief note within 48 hours
