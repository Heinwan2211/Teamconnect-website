Section 1: Facilitator Preparation Guide

Experience Overview and Objectives

Mental & Corporate Wellness is a structured wellness immersion that guides participants through a circuit of five evidence-based stations: a fynbos sensory walk, a stress-mapping workshop, a peer-coaching framework session, a digital detox creative activity, and a breathwork and grounding practice. Facilitated by a professional wellness facilitator, the experience closes with wellbeing commitment cards and a team resilience charter — a co-created document that articulates the team's shared commitment to psychological health. This experience is designed not as entertainment but as genuine investment in team wellbeing.

Primary objectives:

  • Provide genuine psychological relief for teams experiencing burnout, high pressure, or post-change fatigue
  • Build a shared team vocabulary around stress, resilience, and wellbeing
  • Introduce practical, evidence-based wellbeing tools that participants can use independently
  • Create psychological safety for honest conversation about workplace pressure and mental health
  • Produce a team resilience charter as a tangible, ongoing reference for team wellbeing practice

Ideal Group Size and Duration

  • Minimum: 10 participants
  • Maximum: 100 participants (with parallel station facilitation)
  • Optimal group size: 20–50 participants
  • Station rotation group size: 4–8 per group
  • Total duration: Half-day (4 hours) or full day (7 hours with extended stations and lunch)
    • Half-day format: 5 stations × 30 minutes each + transitions + opening + closing = approximately 4 hours
    • Full-day format: 5 stations × 50 minutes each + extended lunch break + team resilience charter workshop = approximately 7 hours

Venue and Space Requirements

  • Any indoor or semi-outdoor venue — some stations benefit from natural settings
  • Fynbos sensory walk station: Requires outdoor access — a garden, courtyard, or nearby natural area. If fully indoor, a curated sensory table with fynbos plant specimens, essential oils, and natural textures can substitute.
  • Stress-mapping station: Quiet indoor breakout room with wall space for posting visual maps (sticky notes, flip chart)
  • Peer-coaching station: Comfortable seating arrangement in pairs or small clusters — encourage a lounge-style setup if available
  • Digital detox creative station: Natural light preferred; table space for art materials
  • Breathwork station: Quiet, calm space with enough floor area for participants to lie down if needed; yoga mats highly recommended
  • Overall atmosphere should feel restful and intentional — avoid fluorescent lighting, loud music, or cluttered tables
  • Consider diffusing calming essential oils throughout the venue (lavender, eucalyptus) — check for allergies

Equipment and Materials Checklist

Team Connect provides:

  • Fynbos sensory walk: collected fynbos specimens (or essential oil substitutes), sensory prompt cards, guided walk script, identification cards for plants
  • Stress-mapping station: large stress-mapping template posters (one per table), coloured markers, stress response reference cards, sticky notes
  • Peer-coaching station: coaching framework cards (GROW model or equivalent), coaching question prompt cards, private reflection journals per participant
  • Digital detox creative station: art materials (watercolour sets, drawing paper, pencils, journals), creative prompt cards, optional guided audio (ambient nature sounds)
  • Breathwork station: yoga mats (or instruct participants to bring), guided breathwork script, grounding exercise cards, optional essential oil roller bottles as takeaways
  • Wellbeing commitment cards (one per participant)
  • Team resilience charter template (large format, one per team/table)
  • PA system and microphone for opening and closing
  • Soft background music playlist for transitions and creative station
  • Printed session flow guide for each station facilitator

Client/venue provides:

  • Tables and comfortable seating
  • Access to outdoor area (for fynbos walk or alternative arrangement)
  • Power access for music/ambient audio at breathwork station

Setup Instructions (Step by Step)

  1. Arrive 90 minutes before participants.
  2. Set up each station individually — atmosphere matters for this experience; each station should feel purposefully designed.
  3. Fynbos sensory walk: lay out specimens, label cards, and sensory prompt cards along the walking path (or at the sensory table if indoor). Test the walk route for safety and comfort.
  4. Stress-mapping station: post template posters on the wall or lay flat on tables. Place coloured markers and sticky notes at each seat.
  5. Peer-coaching station: arrange seating in pairs or circles of 4. Place coaching framework cards and reflection journals at each seat.
  6. Digital detox creative station: lay out art materials with generous space per person. Set up ambient audio (test at comfortable volume). Post creative prompt cards.
  7. Breathwork station: lay out yoga mats with adequate spacing. Test ambient audio. Dim lighting if possible. Place grounding cards near each mat.
  8. Set up central gathering area for opening and closing (comfortable seating for full group).
  9. Place wellbeing commitment cards and team resilience charter templates at facilitator station (distributed during closing).
  10. Test PA system.
  11. Brief all station facilitators on their session content, facilitation approach, and timing.
  12. Final atmosphere check: lighting, scent, music levels, clutter.

Facilitator Briefing Notes

  • The wellness immersion demands a specific facilitation energy: calm, grounded, unhurried. Facilitators who carry stress or urgency into the room will undermine the experience.
  • Each station facilitator should be briefed on trauma-informed facilitation basics: hold space without judgement, never push participants to share more than they choose to, normalise all emotional responses.
  • The stress-mapping station may surface genuine distress — facilitators must be prepared to respond compassionately and, if necessary, know the referral pathway (company EAP or HR contact) for participants who disclose serious mental health concerns.
  • The peer-coaching station requires facilitators who understand coaching principles — do not allow the session to become advice-giving or problem-solving.
  • The fynbos sensory walk is a unique South African element — facilitators should be knowledgeable about fynbos species and their sensory properties. If outdoor access isn't possible, the indoor sensory table must be set up thoughtfully to preserve the intention.
  • The breathwork station requires a facilitator with actual breathwork training or significant lived practice. Do not assign this station to an untrained facilitator.
  • Check for all health conditions and medication that may contraindicate certain breathwork exercises (hyperventilation-style breathing is not appropriate for all participants).

Weather/Contingency Considerations

  • Fynbos walk cancelled by rain: Switch to indoor sensory table arrangement. Notify client in advance if forecast shows high rain probability so they can communicate to participants.
  • Participant discloses mental health crisis: Have EAP or HR contact available. Do not attempt therapeutic intervention — follow referral protocol.
  • Participant declines to participate in any station: This is fully acceptable in a wellness experience. No participant should ever be pressured to engage. They may observe, rest, or opt for a journal reflection alternative.
  • Station facilitator illness day-of: Lead facilitator runs the station at reduced depth — prioritise peer-coaching and breathwork over the others if one facilitator is unavailable.

Pre-Event Client Communication Checklist

  • Confirm participant count and group rotation size
  • Confirm format: half-day or full day
  • Confirm outdoor access for fynbos walk (or confirm indoor sensory alternative)
  • Confirm whether yoga mats are provided by venue/client or by Team Connect
  • Ask client to flag any known mental health conditions, allergies (essential oils, plants), or physical limitations
  • Confirm whether company EAP number should be displayed/distributed at the event
  • Send participant information sheet (Section 5)
  • Confirm whether team resilience charter output should be typed and returned to client post-event

Section 2: Hosting Guide

Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

"Good morning / afternoon.

[Pause — let the room settle.]

Thank you for being here. I mean that genuinely.

We live and work in a world that constantly rewards output. More output. Faster output. Better output. And somewhere in the middle of that — the people producing the output get forgotten.

Today, for [four hours / a full day], the only output we're asking of you is this: to arrive. To be present. To pay attention to what's happening inside you — not just around you.

We've designed five experiences for you today. Each one is grounded in evidence. Each one is designed to give you something you can take home — a practice, a perspective, a tool. None of them require you to be anything other than exactly who you are right now.

There is one gentle guideline: this is a device-free experience from this moment forward. Not because we don't trust you with your phone, but because the whole point of today is to discover what's there when the screen isn't.

You'll be in small groups. The people in your group are not your audience. They're your fellow travellers. Respect their process. Hold what they share with care.

Take a breath.

[Pause — model a slow breath.]

We begin in a moment."

Rules Explanation Script

"A few gentle guidelines for today:

One — devices away, please. This is your protected time. Everything else can wait.

Two — participation is always voluntary. At every station, you choose how much you engage and how much you share. There is no wrong level of participation.

Three — what is shared in the room stays in the room. This is a space of confidentiality and respect.

Four — if at any point today you feel overwhelmed, or you need space — just let your station facilitator know. You can step outside, journal quietly, or simply breathe. You will not be asked to explain.

Five — move through the stations as a group. Your group's pace is the right pace. Don't rush.

Your group assignment is on the card you received. Your first station begins in five minutes.

Take care of each other today."

Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

  • Opening (0–20 min): The energy at opening is often fragile — participants may arrive hurried, distracted, or sceptical. Don't match that energy. Slow down, speak quietly, hold pauses.
  • Fynbos sensory walk: Naturally quietens and grounds people. Walk slowly, speak minimally. Let silence do the work.
  • Stress-mapping station: Energy picks up slightly as people engage cognitively. Keep voices soft. Ensure the space feels private.
  • Peer-coaching station: This station carries the most emotional depth. Hold space. Resist the urge to fill silence. Trust the process.
  • Digital detox creative station: Energy is quiet and reflective. Play soft instrumental or nature audio. Resist narrating or directing creativity.
  • Breathwork station: Most participants will feel noticeably different after this station. The energy should be soft, still, and warm. Allow extended time after the breathing exercise before transitioning.
  • Closing: This is not a high-energy close. It is a ceremonial, intentional close. Speak slowly. Honour the work done.

How to Handle Reluctant Participants

  • Reluctance in a wellness setting often indicates that the experience is touching something real — this is not a problem to solve, it's information to respect.
  • Never apply social pressure or call out reluctant participants.
  • Offer journal reflection as an alternative at any station.
  • At the peer-coaching station: reluctant participants can take the coaching role (listener) rather than the coachee (speaker).
  • In the breathwork station: simply lying quietly is a valid form of participation.

How to Manage Dominant Participants

  • Dominant participants in a wellness context often default to intellectualising or advice-giving.
  • At the peer-coaching station: redirect explicitly — "In coaching, we ask questions rather than offer solutions. Can you try a question?"
  • At the stress-mapping station: if one person dominates the group's map: "Let's make sure we've heard from everyone before we step back and look at the full picture."
  • Redirect dominant energy to the journaling reflection component.

Common Problems and How to Solve Them

Safety Considerations

  • Breathwork: never use hyperventilation-based breathing techniques without explicit facilitator training. Box breathing, 4-7-8 breathing, and simple diaphragmatic breathing are appropriate defaults.
  • Essential oils: confirm no allergies or sensitivities in advance. Diffusion is gentler than direct skin application.
  • Fynbos walk: check path for uneven ground, insects, or tripping hazards. Sunscreen reminder if outdoors.
  • Mental health disclosures: have company EAP number and HR contact readily accessible. Do not improvise crisis support.
  • Confirm first aid contact at venue.

Section 3: Debrief Guide

Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

The wellness immersion is experiential and personal — without a structured debrief, participants leave with individual reflections that never become shared team understanding. The debrief and resilience charter workshop converts personal wellbeing insights into collective team commitments — which is what gives the experience lasting organisational value.

Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

  • Integrated into the resilience charter workshop at the close of the experience.
  • Duration: 30–40 minutes (debrief woven into the charter creation process)
  • Format: Whole group, reflective, with pair sharing moments.

10 Debrief Questions (From Surface to Deep)

  1. "Which station stayed with you most — and what did it give you?"
  2. "What did the fynbos walk remind you of that you've been forgetting?"
  3. "In the stress-mapping session — what surprised you about your own stress map?"
  4. "Was there a question your peer coach asked you that you're still sitting with? You don't have to share the answer — just the fact of the question."
  5. "What did it feel like to put the device away for a few hours? What came up in that space?"
  6. "What does your body feel like right now compared to when you walked in today?"
  7. "What does 'resilience' mean to you — personally, not as a corporate concept?"
  8. "What does your team need from each other to stay well — especially during high-pressure periods?"
  9. "What does your organisation do well to support wellbeing — and where is the gap that your team can close for itself?"
  10. "If your team made one shared commitment to collective wellbeing that you actually kept — what would it be?"

How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

  • The stress-mapping session surfaces what participants actually find stressful — often different from what management assumes. Naming this creates empathy and practical solutions.
  • The peer-coaching station teaches the difference between advice-giving and genuine listening — directly applicable to every 1:1, team meeting, and performance conversation.
  • The digital detox station demonstrates that creativity and clarity are already present when distraction is removed — a direct challenge to the always-on workplace norm.
  • The breathwork station gives participants a physiological reset tool they can use in any high-pressure meeting, presentation, or conflict — a transferable, evidence-based skill.
  • The resilience charter creates shared accountability for team wellbeing — something teams can return to, update, and honour.

How to Close the Debrief Powerfully

"What you've done today takes more courage than any race or puzzle or presentation.

You slowed down. You looked inward. You let someone listen to you. And then you breathed.

In a world that rewards speed and output, those are radical acts.

Your resilience charter [gesture to it] is not a corporate document. It's a promise you made to each other — in this room, today. The only way it stays alive is if you choose to return to it.

Keep your wellbeing commitment card. Read it on a difficult day. Share it with someone you trust.

[Pause]

You deserve to be well. Not just productive. Well.

Thank you for today."

What to Leave the Group With

  • A completed wellbeing commitment card (personal, private)
  • A co-created team resilience charter (group document, displayed and photographed for reference)
  • At least one evidence-based wellbeing tool they can use independently (breathing exercise, stress-mapping framework, coaching questions)
  • Optional: Team Connect can type and return the resilience charter as a formatted document post-event

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this mandatory participation, or can I opt out of specific stations?
All participation is voluntary. At every station, you choose how much you engage and how much you share. Your facilitator will never push you beyond your comfort.

I'm not a "wellness person" — is this for me?
Yes. The experience is designed to work without any prior wellness practice or belief. You don't need to meditate, love nature, or be emotionally expressive. Simply showing up and staying open is enough.

Will I have to share personal information in front of colleagues?
No. What you share is entirely your choice. The peer-coaching station involves a structured one-on-one conversation, not a group disclosure. Full group sharing is never required.

Is breathwork safe for everyone?
The breathwork techniques used are gentle and evidence-based (box breathing, diaphragmatic breathing). If you have any respiratory condition or take medication that may be relevant, please inform your event coordinator in advance.

Will this be recorded or reported back to my employer?
No. Everything that happens in the room is confidential between participants and facilitators.
