# Facilitator Guide: Incentive & Fun Events
### High-Energy Celebration Events | Tournaments, Year-End Functions, Incentive Rewards | 20–1000+ Participants | Variable Duration | Indoor and Outdoor

---

## === SECTION 1: FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE ===

### Experience Overview and Objectives

Incentive & Fun Events are high-energy celebration experiences designed to reward, recognise, and energise large groups. These events span competitive tournaments, year-end functions, incentive reward experiences, and large-scale entertainment activations. Unlike structured learning programmes, Incentive & Fun Events prioritise energy, entertainment, and positive emotion. The primary measure of success is a room full of people who leave feeling celebrated, connected, and proud to be part of the organisation. The facilitator's role is to be a masterful host — equal parts MC, energy manager, and crowd coordinator.

**Primary objectives:**
- Celebrate team and individual achievement
- Create memorable shared experiences that strengthen organisational identity
- Reward and recognise top performers through prizes, ceremonies, and spotlight moments
- Generate positive emotional associations with the company and its culture
- Energise large groups through high-production entertainment and competition
- Create moments of joy and connection across all levels of the organisation

### Ideal Group Size and Duration

- **Minimum:** 20 participants
- **Maximum:** 1000+ participants (larger events require full production team)
- **Optimal group size:** 100–500 participants for standard formats
- **Duration:** Variable — 2 hours (standalone tournament) to full-day or evening events (year-end functions)
- **Team structure:** Varies by format — competitive teams, individual participation, or free-flowing social formats

### Venue and Space Requirements

**Indoor events:**
- Large function rooms, ballrooms, or convention centres for 100+ participants
- Clear sightlines to stage or main performance area
- Dance floor or open social space (for year-end functions)
- High-quality sound system — must cover entire space without dead zones
- Stage lighting and spotlights for award ceremonies and performances
- Large screens or LED walls for leaderboard, video content, and prize reveals

**Outdoor events:**
- Confirmed with venue for capacity, shade, and wet weather alternative
- Generator or power source for PA and AV if not available at venue
- Adequate shade, seating, and refreshment stations for participant comfort
- Safety perimeter for tournament areas (fencing or cordons as required)
- Access for catering and production vehicles

**Tournament-specific:**
- Adequate space per playing court or competition area
- Spectator viewing areas clearly defined
- Dedicated stage or podium for award ceremonies

### Equipment and Materials Checklist

**AV and production:**
- Professional-grade PA system (size matched to venue and attendance)
- Wireless handheld and lapel microphones (minimum 2 of each)
- Large screens or LED walls for leaderboard and video content
- Stage lighting — at minimum: front wash, spotlights, coloured effects
- DJ or live music setup (confirm with client — artist or DJ booked separately)
- Video playback system for brand content, highlight reels, or prize reveals
- Confetti cannons or special effects (if approved by venue)

**Tournament-specific equipment:**
- Competition-specific equipment (sport or game dependent — confirm with client)
- Branded team kits or bibs
- Official scoreboard (digital or physical)
- Referee or judge equipment
- Barriers and rope cordon for playing areas

**Recognition and awards:**
- Trophy or award items (confirm with client — crystal, branded, novelty)
- Prize packs for tournament winners
- Certificates (printed and branded)
- Winner podium or stage riser (for ceremonies)
- Branded envelopes for prize reveals

**Logistics:**
- Name tags or lanyards (for large events where identification matters)
- Printed event programme (optional but professional for large year-end functions)
- Goodie bags or welcome packs (if included in client package)
- Branded backdrop or step-and-repeat for photography

### Setup Instructions (Step by Step)

1. Arrive minimum 3 hours before participants for large events (1000+) or 2 hours for standard events.
2. Confirm venue access and production setup time with venue coordinator.
3. Direct production crew to set up AV: PA system, lighting, screens, stage.
4. Set up tournament competition areas — confirm layout against pre-approved floor plan.
5. Sound check: all microphones tested at performance volume.
6. Video playback test: confirm all content plays correctly on screens.
7. Set up registration or welcome area (if guests are arriving at different times).
8. Confirm catering setup and service timing with catering coordinator.
9. Brief all co-facilitators, assistants, and production crew on roles and cues.
10. Confirm prize and award table placement — secure items out of participant reach until ceremony.
11. Prepare run of show document — review with all team members 30 minutes before doors open.
12. 15 minutes before start: be visible, energetic, and welcoming at the entrance or stage area.

### Facilitator Briefing Notes

**Energy management is the primary skill for these events.** Everything else — timing, prizes, competition — supports the energy, not the other way around.

- Know the run of show in detail: every segment, every cue, every transition.
- Work with the production team to synchronise music, lighting, and microphone handovers.
- MC technique for large groups: short sentences, clear articulation, deliberate pausing. Never talk over music or compete with the room's noise floor.
- Build to peaks deliberately — the prize-giving ceremony should be the loudest, most energised moment of the event.
- Manage downtime aggressively: dead air kills energy. Every gap should be filled with music, content, or interaction.
- Personalise recognition moments: use names, departments, and specific achievements when presenting awards.
- Coordinate with client sponsor on VIP moments — executive speeches, senior leader presentations, personal recognition.

### MC Techniques for Large Groups

- **Project authority from the first word.** A hesitant opening undermines the entire event.
- **Use audience call-and-response to warm the room.** *"If you're here for [COMPANY NAME], make some noise!"*
- **Direct movement explicitly.** Large groups need clear instructions: *"Right now, I need everyone to move to the [AREA]. You have 60 seconds."*
- **Build suspense before prize reveals.** Pause, scan the room, lower your voice before you announce — then explode.
- **Acknowledge the occasion.** Year-end events are emotional — recognise the year, the challenges, and the achievements with genuine warmth.
- **Keep segments tight.** Large audiences lose focus fast. Every segment should end before the room wants it to.

### Prize-Giving Ceremonies

- Prepare a script for every award — name, achievement, and why it matters.
- Allow enough time for winner to reach the stage — do not rush the moment.
- Use music and lighting cues to elevate the ceremony.
- For large volumes of awards, group into categories to maintain energy.
- Always end the ceremony on the highest-value award — the final prize should be the moment everyone remembers.

### Weather/Contingency Considerations

- **Rain (outdoor events):** Confirm wet weather alternative venue in writing before event. Have a clear decision time — typically 2 hours before start.
- **Power failure:** Generator backup for PA system is non-negotiable for large events. Confirm with venue.
- **AV failure:** Have a battery-powered backup PA (even small) for announcements. Maintain crowd management if main sound system fails.
- **Low attendance:** Compress the space to maintain energy density — spread-out crowds feel flat.
- **Over-served participants:** Have a plan for managing intoxicated behaviour at social events. Know the venue's security contacts.
- **Entertainment cancellation:** Have a backup plan for DJ or performer no-shows — a playlist and laptop is a minimum fallback.

### Pre-Event Client Communication Checklist

- [ ] Confirm venue, catering, and production suppliers

## === SECTION 2: HOSTING GUIDE ===

### Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

> *"[PAUSE — wait for silence or applause to settle — THEN begin]*
>
> *Good [EVENING / AFTERNOON / MORNING], [COMPANY NAME]!*
>
> *[PAUSE for response — if energy is low, try again: "I said — GOOD EVENING, [COMPANY NAME]!"]*
>
> *My name is [YOUR NAME], and tonight / today, I have one job: to make sure every single one of you leaves here with a memory that lasts longer than tomorrow morning.*
>
> *Tonight / Today is for you. You have earned this. [COMPANY NAME] has brought you here because this year — this team — this result — deserves to be celebrated properly.*
>
> *So here is what we are going to do. We are going to [BRIEFLY OUTLINE: compete / celebrate / recognise our people / let loose / crown champions]. We are going to make noise. We are going to recognise the people who made this year what it was. And then we are going to [CLOSE THE NIGHT / CELEBRATE TOGETHER].*
>
> *Are you ready? Let's go."*

### Rules Explanation Script (For Tournament Events)

> *"Before we kick off the competition, let me give you the rules.*
>
> *[INSERT TOURNAMENT-SPECIFIC RULES — format, scoring, timing, referee authority.]*
>
> *The referees' decisions are final. Play hard, play fair, play with the same energy you bring to your actual work.*
>
> *Prizes are real. Competition is real. But this is also one day — so play to win and remember why you're here.*
>
> *Any questions? Good. Let the games begin."*

### Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

- **Arrival and welcome:** High energy, warm and personal. Play music at welcome level — loud enough to signal a party, not so loud guests can't speak to each other.
- **Competition / activity segments:** Let the event energy run — step back and let participants lead. Your job is to maintain pace, update scores, and build tension.
- **Downtime between segments:** Keep music going. Do not allow silence. Use this time for audience interaction: *"Who's feeling confident right now? Who thinks their team is taking home that trophy?"*
- **Award ceremony build-up:** Slow the event down deliberately before the ceremony begins. Signal the shift: *"In [X] minutes, we find out who takes home the top prize tonight. Before we get there — [TRANSITION ACTIVITY]."*
- **Award ceremony:** Maximum production value. Music, lighting, full stage presence. Make every winner feel like a star.
- **Close:** End on a high. Leave the room wanting more, not sighing with relief.

### How to Handle Reluctant Participants

- At celebration events, reluctance often looks like a person standing at the edge of the room with a drink, not engaging.
- Do not spotlight or embarrass reluctant participants.
- Use movement and activity to draw people in: *"Everyone, grab a partner — we're doing a 60-second challenge before we announce the next prize."*
- Create easy entry points — group challenges with low stakes allow quiet participants to participate without pressure.

### How to Manage Dominant Participants

- Dominant energy is generally welcome at high-energy events — redirect, not suppress.
- If a participant is disrupting others' experience (heckling, monopolising, creating negative energy): approach privately and redirect with warmth.
- For tournament events, delegate rule enforcement to referees, not the MC.

### Timing Guide (Minute by Minute)

| Time | Action |
|------|--------|
| T-180 min | Production setup begins (large events) |
| T-90 min | AV and PA check complete |
| T-60 min | All staff briefed; venue ready |
| T-30 min | Doors open; welcome music playing; host visible at entrance |
| 00:00 | Official welcome and opening |
| 00:15 | Competition / activity begins or first programme segment |
| Per segment | Score updates, energy calls, audience interaction between segments |
| As programmed | Award ceremony — position at programme peak |
| Programme end | Close and send-off |

### Common Problems and How to Solve Them

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Sound system failure | Move to backup PA for announcements; communicate calmly; continue with modified energy |
| Award winner not present | Have client sponsor present award to next runner-up; hold winner's award for separate delivery |
| Competition goes over time | Compress remaining rounds by reducing time limits; announce the change with energy and humour |
| Entertainment / DJ cancels | Deploy backup playlist; MC more frequently to fill the space |
| Group energy is low at start | Do a call-and-response, group challenge, or quick physical energiser immediately |
| Prize list error | Reconfirm with client sponsor privately before announcing; never announce something you are unsure of |

### Safety Considerations

- For events with 200+ participants: confirm security plan with venue, including crowd management and emergency exit procedures.
- For alcohol-service events: monitor intoxication levels; coordinate with venue on service cutoff if needed.
- For outdoor events: heat, sun exposure, and hydration are facilitator responsibilities to monitor.
- For physical tournaments: confirm warm-up procedures and first aid availability.
- Have emergency contact list: client sponsor, venue security, first aid, production crew lead.

## === SECTION 3: DEBRIEF GUIDE ===

### Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

Incentive & Fun Events are primarily about celebration rather than structured learning — and the debrief reflects that. At these events, a formal debrief is often inappropriate and may deflate the energy. Instead, the "debrief" for these events is typically a brief, warm, purpose-driven close that:

- Acknowledges the achievement being celebrated
- Names the values or qualities being recognised
- Connects the celebration to the organisation's future
- Sends participants away feeling proud and energised

For tournament and competition-focused events, a brief competitive debrief can add meaning to the experience without dampening the energy.

### Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

- **Year-end / celebration events:** 5–10 minutes maximum — a brief close integrated into the ceremony, not a separate debrief session.
- **Tournament / competitive events:** 10–15 minutes after the final award ceremony.
- **Incentive reward events:** Often no formal debrief — the recognition itself is the close.

### Closing Remarks Script for Celebration Events

> *"Before I let you loose for the rest of the evening — one moment.*
>
> *What you've achieved this year is not an accident. It is the result of every person in this room making a choice, day after day, to show up. To contribute. To carry more than their share when it was needed.*
>
> *[COMPANY NAME] sees that. Tonight is the proof.*
>
> *So take this evening, this recognition, this moment — and remember it the next time it gets hard. Because it will get hard. And the people in this room will be the reason you get through it.*
>
> *Thank you for a remarkable year. Now — let's celebrate the way [COMPANY NAME] knows how."*

### 5 Tournament Debrief Questions (Short Format)

1. *"What do you take away from this competition — about your team, or about yourself?"*
2. *"Who surprised you today — on your team or someone you competed against?"*
3. *"What did it feel like to compete with the same people you work with every day? What did you notice?"*
4. *"What made the difference between winning and not winning today — skill, strategy, or something else?"*
5. *"What do you want to bring back to the office from the way your team competed today?"*

### What to Leave the Group With

- A feeling of genuine celebration and recognition
- The names and achievements of award recipients ringing in the room
- A sense that the organisation values and sees them as individuals
- Energy for the year ahead — or a warm send-off into the break
