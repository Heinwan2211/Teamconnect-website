### WHAT TO EXPECT ON THE DAY

You and your leadership team will arrive to a thoughtfully set-up environment — quiet, focused, and deliberately different from your usual workspace. After a welcome and framing session that sets the context and establishes psychological safety, you'll be divided into small rotation groups of four to eight people.

The heart of the experience is four high-stakes decision rooms, each testing a distinct leadership competency. The first room challenges you to communicate clearly and act decisively under conditions of genuine ambiguity — where the information you have is incomplete and the stakes are real. The second room presents an ethical dilemma that has no easy answer and no single right response. The third room requires you to allocate scarce resources under crisis conditions, where every choice has consequences. The fourth room asks you to lead through resistance — to hold your position, adapt your approach, and bring people with you even when they push back.

In each room, your group makes actual decisions — not theoretical ones — and receives structured feedback from a skilled facilitator immediately after. The reflection is as important as the decision itself.

After all four rooms, the full group reconvenes for an integration debrief. This is where individual insights become shared leadership language — where the experiences of each room are connected to the real challenges your leaders face, and where the day becomes a genuine catalyst for how your team leads going forward.

### FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

**Q: Is this suitable for newly promoted managers or more experienced leaders?**
Both. The scenarios are calibrated for the level of the group — we'll discuss your participants' experience levels in advance and ensure the decision rooms are appropriately challenging. The experience is equally valuable for emerging leaders building their foundations and senior leaders examining their established patterns.

**Q: Will participants be judged or assessed during the experience?**
No. This is a development experience, not an assessment. The feedback is designed to be useful and growth-oriented, not evaluative. Participants are encouraged to take risks and be honest, knowing the room is a safe space for that.

**Q: What does "structured feedback" look like in practice?**
After each decision room, a facilitator guides a brief reflection conversation using a structured framework. Participants share what they decided, why, and what they noticed about the group's process. The facilitator highlights patterns and offers observations — not judgements.

**Q: Can we tailor the decision room scenarios to our industry or company context?**
Yes. We can contextualise the scenarios to reflect your sector, your organisation's current strategic priorities, or specific challenges your leadership team is navigating. Share the relevant context with your Team Connect contact ahead of time.

**Q: What's the ideal group size for this experience?**
The experience works best with 16–32 participants, allowing for groups of 4–8 in each room and a rich full-group debrief. We can accommodate smaller groups from 8 and larger groups up to 50 with additional room facilitators — your Team Connect contact will advise on optimal logistics for your specific group.

### GETTING THE MOST FROM YOUR EXPERIENCE

**Set the right tone in advance.** Let your leadership team know this is a serious investment in their development — not a day off, and not a test. The most valuable experiences happen when participants arrive ready to be honest and open.

**Encourage the senior leaders in the room to model vulnerability.** When the most experienced people in the room are willing to say "I'm not sure" or "that decision didn't land the way I intended," it gives everyone else permission to engage with real honesty.

**Protect the debrief time.** The integration debrief is where individual insights become organisational capability. Don't let logistics or time pressure cut it short — it is the most important hour of the day.

**Create a follow-up structure.** One of the best uses of the shared leadership vocabulary that emerges from this experience is to build it into regular leadership team conversations. Consider scheduling a follow-up session four to six weeks after the experience to revisit what was learned and assess what has changed.
