Facilitator Guide: Amazing Race

Multi-Stage Adventure Race | Teams Race Between Waypoints | 20–1000 Participants | 2–4 Hours | Outdoor and Multi-Location

SECTION 1: FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE

Experience Overview and Objectives

The Amazing Race is a multi-stage adventure race in which teams race between waypoints, cracking codes, completing physical challenges, and navigating terrain across outdoor environments. Unlike the Fugitive's pursuit dynamic, the Amazing Race is a straightforward competition: every team runs the same race, but strategy, communication, and teamwork determine who finishes first. This is Team Connect's most logistically complex large-group outdoor experience — managing 1000 participants across multiple locations simultaneously requires a full facilitation team, robust checkpoint infrastructure, and a reliable scoring system.

Primary objectives:

  • Build high-energy shared experience across large groups
  • Develop navigation and strategic decision-making under time pressure
  • Strengthen team communication during physical and cognitive challenge
  • Create genuine competition with real stakes across all team levels
  • Generate lasting team memories through a distinctive, high-effort shared experience

Ideal Group Size and Duration

  • Minimum: 20 participants (4 teams of 5)
  • Maximum: 1000 participants (with full facilitation and checkpoint infrastructure)
  • Optimal group size: 100–400 participants
  • Team size: 4–8 people per team
  • Total duration: 2–4 hours
    • Pre-race briefing: 20–30 minutes
    • Active race: 90–150 minutes
    • Return and debrief: 30–40 minutes

Checkpoint Setup

Checkpoint setup is the foundation of the Amazing Race. Each checkpoint must be staffed, clearly marked, functional, and positioned correctly within the race course design.

Checkpoint types:

  1. Navigation checkpoints: Teams must navigate to the location using map references, compass directions, or landmark clues. No pre-marked route.
  2. Challenge checkpoints: Teams complete a specific physical or cognitive challenge before receiving their next clue.
  3. Code checkpoints: Teams solve a code, puzzle, or cipher to unlock the location of the next checkpoint.
  4. Speed checkpoints: First team to arrive gains a time advantage; all teams must complete a task before continuing.

Checkpoint staffing requirements:

  • Minimum 1 marshal per checkpoint
  • For challenge checkpoints: minimum 2 marshals
  • Marshal radio communication to race control at all times
  • Checkpoint marshal has authority to: confirm task completion, deny incomplete submissions, and issue next clue
  • All marshals arrive minimum 90 minutes before race start

Checkpoint materials (standard per checkpoint):

  • Challenge task equipment (challenge-specific)
  • Clue envelopes for next leg (one set per team colour/number)
  • Team scoring stamp or token system
  • Marshal instruction sheet
  • Radio or mobile phone
  • First aid kit (sealed)
  • Shelter from sun/rain if checkpoint is exposed

Checkpoint placement rules:

  • No checkpoint on active road or traffic route
  • All checkpoint locations accessible on foot
  • Minimum 50m separation between consecutive checkpoints that could cause convergence
  • Clearly visible from the approach route when within 30m

Challenge Design

Challenges are the heart of the Amazing Race. They must be engaging, achievable, clearly defined, and scalable for large groups.

Challenge design principles:

  • Every challenge must be completable by all fitness levels in the group.
  • Challenges should test a combination of physical coordination, cognitive problem-solving, and team communication.
  • Each challenge should be completable in 5–15 minutes by a focused team.
  • Provide clear pass/fail criteria for the checkpoint marshal.
  • Design challenges that require team coordination, not individual heroics.

Example challenge types:

Physical/coordination:

  • Human knot (teams untangle without releasing hands)
  • Tower building challenge (construct a freestanding structure to minimum height)
  • Blindfolded navigation (one teammate blindfolded, guided by voice)
  • Team carry (transport a full water bucket across a course without spilling)

Cognitive/puzzle:

  • Code decryption (decode an encrypted message using a provided cipher key)
  • Logic puzzle (solve a multi-step logic problem)
  • Map orientation (identify six landmarks on a provided map)
  • Trivia challenge (team answers 10 rapid-fire questions — minimum 7 correct to proceed)

Creative:

  • Lip sync battle (team performs a 60-second performance)
  • Sculpture challenge (build a recognisable object from provided materials)

Timing Management

Timing is the most complex operational element of a large Amazing Race.

Timing system design:

  • Assign each team a unique identifier (number, colour, or code).
  • Use a consistent timing system across all checkpoints: QR code scan, stamp card, or digital platform.
  • Race control maintains live timing for all teams.
  • Checkpoint marshals report team arrivals and departures by radio or platform in real time.
  • Time penalties are assigned at race control, not at checkpoints.

Time penalties:

  • Incomplete challenge attempt: 5-minute penalty
  • Out-of-bounds navigation: 10-minute penalty
  • Skipped checkpoint: disqualification from race (participation continues; team enters exhibition mode)

Wave start system (events over 200 participants):

  • Release teams in waves of 15–20 minutes to prevent checkpoint congestion.
  • Waves are separated by start time; race time begins at each team's individual wave start.
  • Final race results account for wave-start time adjustments.

Scoring System

Standard scoring:

  • Points awarded per checkpoint reached: 10 points per standard checkpoint, 20 points per challenge checkpoint successfully completed.
  • Time bonus: first team to reach the final checkpoint receives 50 bonus points; second team 30 points; third team 20 points.
  • Time penalties deducted from final total.
  • Tiebreaker: if two teams have equal points, the team with fewer time penalties wins.

Managing Large Groups Across Multiple Locations

For events above 200 participants:

  • Divide the course into zones with dedicated zone marshals.
  • Communication hierarchy: Race Control → Zone Marshal → Checkpoint Marshal.
  • Establish an all-teams broadcast channel for safety announcements and race updates.

For events above 500 participants:

  • Dedicated logistics coordinator at race control.
  • Multiple finish points, all reporting to race control simultaneously.
  • Participant tracking system (GPS tracker per team or digital check-in).
  • Medical team on standby (dedicated paramedic or nurse).

Venue and Space Requirements

  • Course area: Open outdoor spaces — parks, sports grounds, nature reserves, coastal paths
  • Base location: A fixed, open area where all participants gather for briefing and return after the race
  • Checkpoint locations: Identified during course design; must be accessible on foot
  • Weather shelter: Confirmed alternative or shelter option for base location in case of rain

Equipment and Materials Checklist

Race control:

  • Laptop with live scoring system
  • Scoreboard display (screen or whiteboard visible to all at base)
  • Radio system: minimum one per zone marshal plus race control
  • Live timing records: printed backup
  • Prize and award items (trophies, medals, certificates)

Per checkpoint:

  • Challenge materials (challenge-specific)
  • Clue envelopes — one set per team
  • Timing system: QR scanner, stamp, or digital device
  • Marshal instruction sheet
  • First aid kit (sealed)
  • Radio or mobile phone

Per team:

  • Team race brief (printed — includes course rules, safety, emergency contact)
  • Map (if navigation challenges included)
  • Team identifier: coloured bib, wristband, or lanyard

Facilitator Briefing Notes

  • The race manages itself through the checkpoint system. Your role during the race is base management, communication, and safety oversight — not hosting.
  • Keep race control lean and functional.
  • Confirm all checkpoint marshals are briefed and in position before the briefing begins.
  • Build tension in the briefing — participants should feel the anticipation of genuine competition.
  • At large events, plan for a staged debrief: immediate results reveal as last team returns, followed by structured debrief 15 minutes later.

Weather/Contingency Considerations

  • Rain: The Amazing Race runs in light rain. Heavy rain or thunder/lightning triggers abort protocol. Decision point: 3 hours before start.
  • Extreme heat: Build mandatory water stations into the course. Shorten the race by removing checkpoints rather than increasing pace.
  • Participant injury: Safety protocol activates at checkpoint level.
  • Timing system failure: Switch to manual timing — marshals record team arrival and departure times in writing.
  • Checkpoint disruption: Race control issues real-time instruction to affected teams via marshal — bypass checkpoint, no penalty.

Pre-Event Client Communication Checklist

  • Course designed and all locations confirmed
  • Challenge designs finalised
  • Checkpoint marshals assigned and briefed
  • Safety risk assessment completed
  • Timing and scoring system confirmed
  • Participant information sheet distributed (Section 5)
  • Team assignments and wave starts prepared
  • Prize and award items confirmed with client
  • Wet weather decision point agreed

SECTION 2: HOSTING GUIDE

Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

"Good [MORNING / AFTERNOON], [COMPANY NAME]. Welcome to the Amazing Race.

In [X] minutes, [NUMBER] teams are going to race across [ZONE NAME]. Between here and the finish line, there are [NUMBER] checkpoints. At each one: a clue, a challenge, or a code standing between your team and the next leg.

This is a race. Not a metaphor. Not a workshop with a fun name. An actual race, with actual points, actual time pressure, and — for the winners — an actual prize.

The teams that win today will not be the fittest. They will be the best at three things: navigating together, communicating under pressure, and making fast decisions with incomplete information.

Sound familiar? It should.

Before we start — your race brief is in your hands. Read the safety rules now. [Pause — 30 seconds.] Specifically: stay in bounds, road crossings at pedestrian crossings only, emergency number is [NUMBER], and if you hear [ABORT SIGNAL] — stop and return to base immediately.

Team [TEAM 1] — you're in Wave 1. You leave in [X] minutes.

Good luck. You're going to need every second."

Rules Explanation Script

"Rules — briefly.

One: every team completes every checkpoint. Skipping a checkpoint is an immediate disqualification from the race standings.

Two: challenge tasks must be completed to the marshal's satisfaction. Partial completion earns a time penalty, not a pass.

Three: your route is defined by your clues. Out-of-bounds movement earns a 10-minute penalty.

Four: the emergency number is on your brief card. Put it in your phone now.

Five: the race ends when the time limit is reached or your team crosses the finish checkpoint. Return to base immediately when your race is done.

Questions? [Pause.] Wave 1 — you're up."

Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

  • Briefing: Urgent, competitive, electric. This is the moment to launch teams into the race with maximum energy.
  • During the race: Facilitator manages base. Keep music playing at base — creates energy for returning teams.
  • As first teams return: Immediately announce their return over PA — "Team [NAME] has just crossed the finish line — they're on the board!"
  • Results reveal: Maximum drama. Pause before each placing. Use music. Call teams to the front.
  • Debrief: Transition smoothly from competitive energy to reflective mode.

How to Handle Reluctant Participants

  • Frame the race around strategy, not fitness: "The slowest team on foot can win if they solve challenges faster than everyone else."
  • Assign challenge roles that do not require physical speed (navigator, code-breaker, team communicator).
  • If a participant is genuinely unable to participate physically, create a base support role.

How to Manage Dominant Participants

  • Racing naturally produces self-organising leadership — allow it to emerge.
  • If one person is making all decisions and suppressing the team: this becomes valuable debrief material. Note it and bring it to the reflection.
  • Checkpoint marshals should not intervene in team dynamics during challenges — unless safety is involved.

  • For events above 200 participants: dedicated safety lead separate from race control coordinator.
  • Medical support confirmed in advance (paramedic or nurse for events above 400 participants).

  • Checkpoint marshals carry sealed first aid kits and are trained to call for support, not treat independently.

SECTION 3: DEBRIEF GUIDE

Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

The Amazing Race generates clear, visible performance data: which teams communicated well, which panicked, which over-thought and fell behind, which trusted their navigation and flew. The debrief turns this data into insight — not just about team performance today, but about team performance at work.

Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

  • Allow 15 minutes after the last team returns for recovery and results confirmation.
  • Duration: 30–40 minutes
  • Format: Whole group for results reveal and opening debrief; small team reflection; whole group synthesis.

10 Debrief Questions (From Surface to Deep)

  1. "Before the results — tell me about one moment from your race today. One specific moment that you'll remember. Go."
  2. "What was your team's strategy at the start? Did you stick to it? What made you change it?"
  3. "Which checkpoint gave you the most trouble — and how did your team respond when it was hard?"
  4. "Who led today? Was it the same person all the way through, or did leadership shift? What drove that shift?"
  5. "At what point did your team communicate best? What were you doing differently in that moment?"
  6. "At what point did communication break down? What was happening in the team?"
  7. "Did your team make any decisions you regret? What would you do differently?"
  8. "Looking at the result — does it reflect how your team actually performed, or does the score miss something?"
  9. "When does this same dynamic — the navigation, the time pressure, the need to make fast decisions together — show up in your work?"
  10. "What's the one thing you want to take from today's race into how your team works this week?"

How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

  • Navigation mirrors project planning: teams that mapped their approach before starting typically performed better than those who just ran.
  • Challenge completion under time pressure mirrors deadline work: the team that communicated clearly solved challenges faster regardless of individual skill level.
  • The teams that lost time at checkpoints often had a communication failure, not a skill failure.
  • The physically fastest individuals did not always win: the teams that aligned quickly and made collective decisions consistently out-performed individual sprinters.

How to Close the Debrief Powerfully

"You've just raced [DISTANCE / COURSE DESCRIPTION] with your team. You navigated, you competed, you made decisions that cost you time or saved you time — and you found out something real about how this group works.

The results are on the board. Some of you are proud. Some of you are frustrated. Both of those are useful.

But here's what I want to leave you with. The teams that performed best today didn't have the fittest people. They had the best communication. Every single checkpoint that separated the top teams from the rest came down to one thing: did this group decide together and move together, or did they fragment?

[PAUSE]

Take 60 seconds. Write down one thing your team will do differently — not in a race, but at work. One communication behaviour. One decision-making habit. Something specific.

That's your race result. Everything else is just the leaderboard."

What to Leave the Group With

  • A written, specific behavioural commitment
  • Named team strengths and named team challenges from the race
  • The shared language of the race as a reference point

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to be fit?
The Amazing Race is designed for all fitness levels. It involves walking — not running — across outdoor terrain. Teams with mixed fitness levels can and do win.

What if the weather is bad?
The race runs in light rain. In the event of severe weather, your event coordinator will communicate a contingency plan by [INSERT TIME].

What if my team gets lost?
A zone map is provided to your team. Marshals are positioned throughout the course. If your team is unsure of your location, call the emergency number.
