# Facilitator Guide: Leadership Development
### Scenario-Based Leadership Lab | High-Stakes Decision Rooms | Half-Day | 8–50 Participants | Any Venue

---

## === SECTION 1: FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE ===

### Experience Overview and Objectives

Leadership Development is a scenario-based leadership laboratory where participants rotate through four high-stakes decision rooms, each testing a distinct leadership competency: communication under ambiguity, ethical decision-making, crisis resource allocation, and leading through resistance. Each room presents a realistic scenario that mirrors real pressures leaders face. Participants work in small groups, make actual decisions, and receive structured feedback after each room. The experience is designed for senior leadership, newly promoted managers, or high-potential talent cohorts.

**Primary objectives:**
- Stretch leadership thinking in a safe, structured environment
- Surface individual leadership styles and their impact on team decisions
- Build fluency in four critical leadership domains: ambiguity, ethics, resource constraints, and resistance
- Create shared leadership language and reference points for future coaching conversations
- Accelerate leadership development without requiring an off-site retreat programme

### Ideal Group Size and Duration

- **Minimum:** 8 participants (groups of 2 per room, rotating)
- **Maximum:** 50 participants (groups of 10–12, with multiple room facilitators)
- **Optimal group size:** 16–32 participants
- **Rotation group size:** 4–8 per group
- **Total duration:** Half-day (4–5 hours)
  - Welcome and framing: 20 minutes
  - Room rotations (4 rooms × 30 minutes each): 120 minutes
  - Rotation transitions: 15 minutes total (4 × ~4 minutes)
  - Lunch or break: 30 minutes (optional, mid-programme)
  - Full-group integration debrief: 45 minutes
  - Individual reflection and commitment close: 20 minutes

### Venue and Space Requirements

- **Any indoor venue** — four separate breakout rooms or clearly separated zones in a single large space
- Each "decision room" needs:
  - A table with 4–8 chairs
  - Wall or easel space to display the scenario
  - Writing materials (pens, sticky notes, flipchart paper)
  - A clock or timer visible to participants
- A central gathering area for group assembly, transitions, and closing debrief
- If using a single large room: use screens, partitions, or simply space the four zones well apart
- PA system not essential if space allows natural voice projection — microphone helpful for the opening and closing in full-group mode
- One room facilitator per decision room (4 facilitators total for optimal delivery — minimum: lead facilitator rotates with one assistant)

### Equipment and Materials Checklist

**Team Connect provides:**
- Scenario brief cards (one per room, laminated or printed — one pack per group)
- Room decision journals (one per participant — notepad format)
- Room role cards (optional: assigns specific leadership roles per scenario)
- Facilitator score/observation sheets (one per room facilitator)
- Transition prompt cards (given to participants as they enter each new room)
- Timer (per room) or central chime system for transitions
- Full-group debrief facilitation materials: whiteboard or flip chart, markers
- Leadership framework reference card (one per participant — given at the start)
- Closing commitment card (one per participant)
- PA system (for opening and closing if space requires)

**Client/venue provides:**
- Breakout rooms or separated zones
- Tables and chairs per room
- Flipchart stands or whiteboards per room (recommended)

### Setup Instructions (Step by Step)

1. Arrive 90 minutes before participants.
2. Set up each decision room independently:
   - Place scenario brief cards face down on the table.
   - Place room role cards at chairs (if using).
   - Set up observation sheet at room facilitator's station.
   - Confirm timer per room.
   - Flip chart or whiteboard ready with markers.
3. Set up central gathering area: enough seating for full group, facilitator position clear, flip chart for opening.
4. Place leadership framework reference cards and room decision journals at the central gathering area — given to participants on arrival.
5. Place transition prompt cards in sealed envelopes at each room exit (participants pick up on leaving).
6. Test PA system if used.
7. Brief all room facilitators on their scenarios, scoring criteria, and facilitation role.
8. Confirm transition timing signal (bell, chime, facilitator call, or phone alarm per room).
9. Print and review participant roster — confirm group assignments for rotations.
10. Prepare closing commitment cards at facilitator station.

### Facilitator Briefing Notes

- This experience requires more facilitator preparation than any other in the portfolio — each room facilitator must know their scenario deeply and be comfortable guiding decision-making without leading it.
- Room facilitators observe, probe with Socratic questions, and score — they do not solve the scenario for participants.
- The four scenarios should increase in emotional difficulty: start with ambiguity (most cognitive), move to ethics and resistance (more emotional), and close with crisis resource allocation (most pressure-intensive).
- The lead facilitator sets the tone in the opening — this should feel more like a professional development investment than a game.
- Feedback after each room should be brief (2–3 minutes per group) and focused on observed behaviour, not evaluation. *"I noticed..."* not *"You should have..."*
- Refer to participants by their actual leadership role where helpful — connecting scenario behaviour to real-world position increases transfer.

### Weather/Contingency Considerations

- Fully indoor — weather is not a primary risk.
- **Facilitator shortage:** Lead facilitator covers two rooms with staggered rotation timing.
- **Participant absence:** Reduce rotation group size; adjust room dynamics accordingly.
- **Tech failure (timer display):** Use phone alarm system; designate a timekeeper per group.
- **Scenario too familiar (e.g., client works in exactly that industry):** Have an alternative scenario from a different sector on hand to avoid overly "easy" answers based on direct experience.

## === SECTION 2: HOSTING GUIDE ===

### Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

> *"Good morning / afternoon. Welcome.*
>
> *[Pause — allow the room to settle.]*
>
> *I want to start today differently from most programmes you've attended. I'm not going to tell you what leadership is. You know what leadership is. You've been doing it — for months, years, maybe decades.*
>
> *What today is about is something harder: looking at how you actually lead, not how you intend to lead.*
>
> *In the next four hours, you're going to walk into four rooms. In each room, there is a scenario. A situation that is incomplete, uncomfortable, and genuinely difficult. There is no single right answer. There is no trick.*
>
> *There is only what you decide, why you decide it, and what your team does when you lead.*
>
> *We have facilitators in every room — not to judge you, but to help you see yourself more clearly. The only thing we ask is that you engage fully. Bring the leader you actually are into each room — not the leader you think we want to see.*
>
> *This is a safe space. What happens in the rooms stays in the rooms — unless you choose to share it in the debrief.*
>
> *Please collect your leadership framework card and your decision journal on the way to your first room. Your group assignment is on the card.*
>
> *We begin in five minutes."*

### Rules Explanation Script

> *"A few guidelines before you begin:*
>
> *One — each room has a scenario. Read it carefully as a group before discussing. Don't rush to solve it.*
>
> *Two — you have 30 minutes in each room. The final 5 minutes are reserved for individual reflection in your journal. When the transition signal sounds, you complete your reflection and move.*
>
> *Three — your room facilitator will ask you questions during the scenario. Answer them honestly. There is no scoring for giving the "right" answer — only for the quality of your thinking.*
>
> *Four — use your leadership framework card as a reference tool in the room — not as a checklist, but as a lens.*
>
> *Five — your decision journal is private. Write what you actually think, not what you'd say in a performance review.*
>
> *Any questions? Then let's go."*

### Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

- **Opening (0–20 min):** Set a tone that is serious, purposeful, and energising — not heavy. The experience should feel like a privilege, not a test.
- **Rotation 1 (20–50 min):** Groups will be cautious in the first room. Room facilitators: allow silence, ask questions, don't fill the space too quickly.
- **Rotation 2 (55–85 min):** Groups are more engaged. The second scenario will provoke stronger disagreement — embrace it.
- **Break (if scheduled mid-programme):** Keep break time to 20–30 minutes. The energy can dissipate quickly — brief the group on what's coming next before they break.
- **Rotations 3 & 4 (resuming):** These are the most challenging rooms — emotional energy runs higher. Room facilitators must be especially attuned.
- **Debrief (full group):** Shift from individual intensity to collective reflection. Start with lighter surface questions before going deep.

### How to Handle Reluctant Participants

- In a leadership programme, reluctance often masks anxiety about being evaluated. Name this directly and compassionately in the opening.
- Assign reluctant participants an anchor role in the first room: *"Could you start us off by reading the scenario aloud to the group?"*
- Avoid creating hierarchy within the participant group — this is a peer experience, regardless of seniority.

### How to Manage Dominant Participants

- Senior leaders may dominate room dynamics and prevent others from contributing.
- Room facilitators use explicit intervention: *"Before we develop that idea further — what does the rest of the group think?"*
- In severe cases, the room facilitator uses the scenario to redirect: *"If you were leading a team through this — and you had a very strong opinion — how would you ensure you were still hearing the team?"* (This is both a redirect and a learning moment.)

### Common Problems and How to Solve Them

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Group agrees too quickly without real debate | Room facilitator introduces a challenge: *"What if that decision backfired — what would you do?"* |
| Group is stuck and not progressing | Redirect with a clarifying question: *"What would change if you had 24 hours to decide instead of 2?"* |
| Senior participant shuts down junior participants | Room facilitator intervenes gently with a direct question to the quieter participant |
| A participant becomes emotionally activated by a scenario | Acknowledge it: *"That's clearly a scenario that resonates — that's a strength, not a weakness. What's coming up for you?"* |
| Time runs out before decision is reached | Facilitator notes the state of the discussion and moves to journal reflection — the incompleteness is itself a data point for debrief |
| Groups are at very different stages across rooms | Adjust transition timing — all groups transition simultaneously regardless of completion |

### Safety Considerations

- Leadership scenarios dealing with ethical dilemmas or crisis situations can trigger genuine emotional responses, particularly for participants who have experienced similar real-world events.
- Room facilitators must be equipped to hold space for emotional responses — brief on this explicitly before the event.
- Confirm with client whether any sensitive topics (retrenchments, leadership failures, discrimination cases) should be avoided in scenario design.
- Confidentiality: be explicit that individual performance is not reported back to management without consent.

## === SECTION 3: DEBRIEF GUIDE ===

### Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

The decision rooms generate dense individual and group experience — multiple leadership scenarios, peer interactions, and self-reflections accumulated across the half-day. Without a structured debrief, participants leave with a collection of isolated observations. The debrief integrates these into shared insight, identifies patterns, and creates conditions for lasting behavioural change.

### Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

- Full-group debrief after the final room transition (and break, if applicable).
- Duration: **40–45 minutes**
- Format: Lead facilitator guides full group; room facilitators may contribute observations (with participant consent).

### 10 Debrief Questions (From Surface to Deep)

1. *"Which room did you find most challenging — and why?"*
2. *"In which room did your group make its best decision? What made that possible?"*
3. *"Did you notice yourself leading differently in different rooms? What changed, and why?"*
4. *"When did you feel most uncertain during the day — and what did you do with that uncertainty?"*
5. *"Was there a moment when you disagreed with your group's direction but went along anyway? What was that like?"*
6. *"When you look at how your group worked across all four rooms — what's the pattern? What does your group consistently do well, and where does it consistently struggle?"*
7. *"Which scenario felt most like a situation you've already faced — or expect to face — in your actual role?"*
8. *"What did you discover about your leadership style today that you didn't already know?"*
9. *"What does your team need from you as a leader that you're not currently giving them consistently?"*
10. *"If you could change one leadership behaviour in the next 30 days — one that your team would actually notice — what would it be?"*

### How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

- The ambiguity room mirrors every strategic decision made without full information — which is most of them. Leaders who thrive in ambiguity have a competitive advantage.
- The ethics room surfaces how teams handle pressure to do the expedient thing vs. the right thing. This is a daily reality in every organisation.
- The resource allocation room mirrors budget cuts, headcount decisions, and project prioritisation — the exact pressures most leaders face in Q3 and Q4 planning cycles.
- The resistance room is a mirror for every change management challenge. Leaders who can lead through resistance without losing the people are the rarest kind.

### How to Close the Debrief Powerfully

> *"You've spent a half-day doing something that most leaders never do: you put yourself in impossible situations on purpose — and you paid attention to what happened.*
>
> *The rooms weren't about being right. They were about being honest. Honest about how you think. How you decide. How you show up when it's uncomfortable.*
>
> *Leadership doesn't improve from reading about it. It improves from doing it, reflecting on it, and doing it differently the next time.*
>
> *Before you leave today, I want you to write one commitment on your card. Not a goal. A behaviour. Something specific that, if you did it consistently for the next 30 days, the people around you would notice a difference.*
>
> *This card is yours. No one will read it unless you choose to share it.*
>
> *Keep it somewhere you'll see it.*
>
> *[Pause]*
>
> *Thank you for doing the difficult work today. It matters."*

### What to Leave the Group With

- A completed commitment card with one specific 30-day behavioural change
- The language of the four leadership domains (ambiguity, ethics, resource constraints, resistance) as a shared reference for future team conversations
- A decision journal filled with genuine reflection notes
- Optional: a printed summary of the leadership framework for ongoing reference

#### Frequently Asked Questions

**Will my performance be reported back to my manager?**
No. The experience is confidential. Room facilitators observe to guide the process — not to evaluate individual participants for management reporting. What happens in the rooms stays in the rooms unless you choose to share.

**Is this like an assessment centre?**
No. An assessment centre evaluates you against a fixed standard. Leadership Development is a developmental experience — the goal is reflection, insight, and growth, not a score or rating.

**What if my group disagrees strongly in one of the rooms?**
That's exactly what's supposed to happen. Productive disagreement is a leadership skill. The room facilitator will help your group work through it — not resolve it for you.

**Do I need to prepare anything in advance?**
No preparation is required. Come as you are. The scenarios are designed to work with your current experience and perspective.

**Is this suitable for new managers as well as senior leaders?**
Yes. The scenarios work across leadership experience levels. In fact, mixed-level groups often produce the richest conversations.
