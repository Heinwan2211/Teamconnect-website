Facilitator Guide: Culture Throwdown

Game-Show-Style Workplace Culture Experience | MC-Hosted | Live Scoreboard | 2–3 Hours | Any Venue

SECTION 1: FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE

Experience Overview and Objectives

Culture Throwdown is a high-energy, MC-hosted game-show experience that unpacks workplace culture through fast-paced competitive rounds. Teams face off through culture trivia, values dilemmas, read-the-room scenarios, and live debates — all scored in real time on a live leaderboard. The experience is both entertaining and genuinely revealing: the discussions that happen during values dilemmas and debate rounds surface real team beliefs, tensions, and shared identity.

Primary objectives:

  • Surface and examine actual team values in action — not just values written on a wall
  • Build shared language around culture, behaviour, and workplace expectations
  • Create psychological safety through laughter and structured debate
  • Energise teams during culture change, onboarding, or strategy alignment moments
  • Celebrate what is great about the company's culture while honestly exploring what needs work

Ideal Group Size and Duration

  • Minimum: 15 participants (3 teams of 5)
  • Maximum: 300 participants
  • Optimal group size: 30–150 participants
  • Team size: 5–8 people per team
  • Total duration: 2–3 hours
    • Setup and warm-up: 15 minutes
    • Rounds 1–4 (main game): 75–100 minutes
    • Break (mid-game or post-game): 15 minutes
    • Final round + awards + debrief: 30–40 minutes

Venue and Space Requirements

  • Any indoor or outdoor venue — flexible
  • Teams grouped at tables or standing pods with enough space for 5–8 per group
  • Clear sightlines to the MC and main scoreboard screen
  • PA system with wireless microphone essential — MC must be heard clearly
  • Scoreboard display: large screen, projector, or TV monitors
  • Optional: handheld buzzers per team (highly recommended)
  • Adequate space for facilitator to move freely between teams during rounds

Equipment and Materials Checklist

Team Connect provides:

  • MC script and question decks (printed and digital backup)
  • Scoreboard software or template
  • Wireless MC microphone and PA system
  • Laptop + HDMI for scoreboard display
  • Team buzzers (if confirmed with client)
  • Team name paddles or table markers
  • Score tracking sheets (paper backup)
  • Timer (digital, displayed or separate device)
  • Prizes for top teams
  • Printed round summary cards (one per team per round — optional but useful for values dilemma rounds)

Setup Instructions (Step by Step)

  1. Arrive at venue 75 minutes before participants.
  2. Set up teams: arrange tables or pods, one per team, facing the MC position and screen.
  3. Set up MC station: mic stand or handheld holder, laptop, PA system.
  4. Load scoreboard software — test all team names display correctly.
  5. Place team name paddles at each table.
  6. If using buzzers: test each buzzer for responsiveness and distinct sound.
  7. Test PA system — walk to the furthest point in the room to confirm audio.
  8. Load timer display.
  9. Brief any scorekeeping assistants on their role.
  10. Print and arrange any round summary cards at facilitator station.
  11. Do a full MC run-through — say opening lines aloud to settle nerves and check mic levels.
  12. Arrange prizes at MC station — not visible to participants until awards.

Facilitator Briefing Notes

  • Culture Throwdown lives or dies on MC energy — commit to the performance from the moment participants enter.
  • Know the client's stated company values before the event — reference them directly during values dilemma rounds.
  • Research any known company context (recent restructure, upcoming merger, new leadership).
  • Be comfortable with productive controversy — values dilemmas are designed to create disagreement.
  • Manage the scoreboard with transparency — teams are competitive and will challenge scores.
  • The debate round requires a confident MC who can cut off speakers, summarise arguments, and award points based on quality of reasoning, not volume.

Pre-Event Client Communication Checklist

  • Confirm company values list (official wording) for round customisation
  • Confirm final participant count and number of teams
  • Confirm team names (client-selected, team-generated on arrival, or MC assigns)
  • Confirm venue AV capability: screen/projector + connectivity
  • Send participant information sheet (Section 5)
  • Confirm prize selection
  • Confirm whether client wants values dilemma questions customised to their company
  • Confirm whether a specific culture theme should be woven through

SECTION 2: HOSTING GUIDE

Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

"WELCOME, everyone — to the Culture Throwdown!

[Pause for energy/applause]

My name is [YOUR NAME] — and for the next [2/3] hours, I am your host, your referee, your judge, and if things get heated — your therapist.

Here's what we're doing today. You're going to compete for the title of [COMPANY NAME]'s most culturally sharp team. This isn't just trivia. This is about what your team actually believes, how you actually behave, and what kind of culture you're building — whether you realise it or not.

We've got four rounds. Each round is different. Some are fast. Some are spicy. One round is going to cause some very interesting conversations between you and the person sitting next to you.

The leaderboard is live. The scores are real. And the prize... is bragging rights and [insert prize here].

Look at your team. Remember their faces now. Because by the end of today, you're either going to love them more — or finally understand why certain meetings go the way they do.

Let's play. Round One starts in thirty seconds."

Rules Explanation Script

"Quick rules before we begin:

One — your team's name is on the board. You win points as a team. No individual scoring.

Two — when a question is asked, your team has [30/60] seconds to agree on an answer. No shouting out before time.

Three — in debate rounds, one representative speaks for your team. Choose wisely — I'm scoring quality of argument, not how loud you are.

Four — my decision on scores is final. You can appeal once per game — but only once.

Five — the golden rule: disagree with ideas, never with people. The values dilemma round is going to test that rule. I'm watching.

Any questions? Then let's get into it."

Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

  • Opening (0–15 min): Pump energy with music as teams settle. Do a quick warm-up question that gets people laughing.
  • Rounds 1–2 (15–60 min): Culture trivia and read-the-room scenarios keep energy consistent. Move quickly between questions.
  • Values dilemma round (60–90 min): Energy shifts to discussion — read the room. Keep pace moving but allow the conversations to breathe.
  • Debate round (90–120 min): Energy spikes again. Use dramatic music stings for team entries. Be theatrical.
  • Awards (final 15 min): Return to high energy. Celebrate all teams, not just the winner.

How to Handle Reluctant Participants

  • Position reluctant participants as the "strategy advisor" — they don't have to speak publicly but they whisper their answer to the team.
  • Ask them a direct question during a trivia round where there's no wrong answer.
  • Avoid calling them out individually on the microphone unless they've signalled openness.

How to Manage Dominant Participants

  • Use the rule: "One spokesperson per team per round — rotate each round."
  • If one person keeps speaking over teammates: "I'm going to need your team's voice here — not just yours. Let's hear from someone else at the table."
  • Redirect energy positively: "I love the confidence — let's see if your team agrees."

Common Problems and How to Solve Them

ProblemSolution
Teams argue about a scoreAcknowledge the dispute, apply the one-appeal rule, move on decisively
Values dilemma causes real tensionValidate the disagreement: "This is exactly why we're here — let's look at both sides"
One team is far ahead on pointsIntroduce a "catch-up round" mechanic (double points for final question)
MC loses the room's energyDrop a surprise twist: a bonus question with an outrageous prize stake

No buzzer — shouted answers create confusionUse hand-raise rule strictly; MC calls the first visible hand

Safety Considerations

  • Values debates can surface genuine cultural tension — facilitator must monitor for personal conflict and redirect to ideas, not people.
  • Ensure no question content is inadvertently discriminatory — review all question sets before the event.
  • Outdoor venues: check for shade, sun exposure, and participant comfort, especially in summer.
  • Confirm first aid contact at venue.

SECTION 3: DEBRIEF GUIDE

Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

Culture Throwdown is deliberately provocative. The values dilemma and debate rounds surface genuine disagreements about workplace behaviour and culture. Without a debrief, teams leave with unresolved tension or half-finished conversations. The debrief converts those conversations into shared understanding and commitment.

Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

  • Debrief after the awards — let the game energy land before reflection begins.
  • Duration: 20–25 minutes
  • Format: Whole group, MC-facilitated, with pair reflection in the middle.

10 Debrief Questions (From Surface to Deep)

  1. "Which round was your team best at — and why do you think that is?"
  2. "Which question or scenario surprised you the most? What was surprising about it?"
  3. "In the values dilemma round — was there a question your team genuinely disagreed on? What was the disagreement?"
  4. "How did your team make decisions during the game? Did someone naturally lead? Did you vote? Did you argue it out?"
  5. "Were there any moments where you had to go with the team's answer even though you personally thought something different? How did that feel?"
  6. "What does how your team played today tell you about how your team works every day?"
  7. "Are the company values we referenced today lived in reality — or are some of them aspirational?"
  8. "What's one cultural behaviour that you think your team does really well?"
  9. "What's one cultural habit your team could honestly improve — and what would that look like in practice?"
  10. "If your team could change one thing about how you work together tomorrow, what would it be?"

How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

  • Read-the-room scenarios mirror the real judgment calls people make in meetings, emails, and client interactions.
  • The values dilemma round often reveals the gap between stated culture and lived culture.
  • The debate round shows how teams argue: who leads, who listens, who gets defensive, who stays curious under challenge.
  • Teams that performed well usually had psychological safety — everyone felt safe to speak, disagree, and revise their view.

How to Close the Debrief Powerfully

"Culture isn't on a poster. Culture is what happens when no one's watching. It's the meeting that starts 10 minutes late. The feedback that never gets given. The brave conversation someone chose not to have.

Today you played a game. But in every round, you showed each other what you actually believe — and that matters far more than any score on the board.

Here's the challenge I'm leaving you with: name one cultural behaviour you're going to bring back to your team on Monday. Not a project. Not a process. A behaviour.

Take 60 seconds. Write it down. Share it with one person at your table.

That's your Culture Throwdown commitment. And your team is your witness."

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to know a lot about our company values?
It helps, but it's not required. The trivia round is broad, and the values rounds are about what you genuinely think — not what you think the right answer is.

What if I'm not comfortable debating in public?
The debate round uses team representatives — your team decides who speaks. You're never put on the spot individually if you don't want to be.

Is this suitable for new employees?
Yes — in fact, Culture Throwdown is one of the best onboarding experiences. New employees often bring fresh perspectives that veteran team members find genuinely valuable.
