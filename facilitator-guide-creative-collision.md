# Facilitator Guide: Creative Collision
### Design Challenge Experience | Shark Tank Meets Maker Lab | AI-Assisted Brainstorming | 3–4 Hours | Indoor

---

## === SECTION 1: FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE ===

### Experience Overview and Objectives

Creative Collision is a design challenge experience where teams receive a real-world brief, ideate, build a prototype solution using unconventional materials, and pitch to a panel of surprise "investors." Think Shark Tank meets maker lab. The experience is deliberately designed to break down the habits of safe, conventional thinking — using AI-assisted brainstorming prompts, time pressure, and unusual materials to force creativity.

**Primary objectives:**
- Break down silo thinking by combining cross-functional or mixed-level teams
- Build comfort with ambiguity, iteration, and rapid decision-making
- Surface creative problem-solving styles and innovation leadership
- Create a shared experience of making, pitching, and defending ideas
- Demonstrate that innovation doesn't require the right background — it requires the right conditions

### Ideal Group Size and Duration

- **Minimum:** 12 participants (3 teams of 4)
- **Maximum:** 120 participants (with multiple investor panels)
- **Optimal group size:** 20–80 participants
- **Team size:** 4–6 people per team
- **Total duration:** 3–4 hours
  - Briefing and warm-up: 20 minutes
  - Ideation sprint: 20 minutes
  - Build phase: 60 minutes
  - Pitch preparation: 20 minutes
  - Pitches and investor Q&A: 45–60 minutes (depending on number of teams)
  - Awards and debrief: 30 minutes

### Venue and Space Requirements

- **Indoor only** — materials are not suitable for outdoor/windy environments
- Open-plan floor space for "maker stations" — each team needs a clear workspace of approximately 4m x 2m
- Long tables (trestle-style) per team to spread out materials
- A dedicated pitch stage area — even if it's just a cleared zone at the front with a mic stand
- Power access at facilitator station for laptop and AI brainstorming display
- Good lighting across all workstations — creative work suffers in dim conditions
- Projector or large screen for brief delivery and investor panel display
- Ample wall or board space for ideas to be pinned or stuck during ideation

**Recommended venue types:** design studios, open-plan offices, makerspace facilities, hotel breakaway rooms with central open area.

### Equipment and Materials Checklist

**Team Connect provides:**
- Real-world brief cards (customised per client or drawn from stock brief library)
- "Maker materials" kit per team:
  - Cardboard sheets, offcuts
  - Tape (masking, duct, double-sided)
  - Scissors and craft knives (facilitator-managed)
  - Markers, pens, sticky notes
  - String, wire, rubber bands
  - Newspaper, magazines (for collage/mood board)
  - Fabric scraps
  - Plastic cups, straws, paper clips
  - A mystery "wildcard" material (changes per event — past examples: modelling clay, LEGO, puzzle pieces)
- AI brainstorming prompt cards (based on ChatGPT/Claude prompts — facilitator reads aloud or displays)
- Timer display
- Pitch evaluation scorecards for investors
- Investor "paddle" cards (thumbs up / thumbs down for TV drama effect — optional)
- Prize certificates
- PA system and wireless microphone
- Laptop + HDMI for brief and scoreboard

**Client/venue provides:**
- Tables and chairs for teams
- Projector or large screen

### Setup Instructions (Step by Step)

1. Arrive 90 minutes before participants.
2. Map out team workstations — clear and generous space per team.
3. Set up brief delivery screen and investor panel area (pitching zone) separately from workstations.
4. Place materials kits at each team workstation — do not let teams touch them until the build phase.
5. Set up PA system and microphone. Test from the pitch zone and from the back of the room.
6. Prepare the brief cards — keep them sealed in envelopes at facilitator station.
7. Load AI brainstorming prompt deck on laptop — or print cards as backup.
8. Brief investor panel (if using external surprise investors — confirm arrival time). If using internal "investors" (e.g., company execs), brief them on their role and the scoring criteria.
9. Set up pitch zone: clear floor area, microphone, display screen for team slides (if participants are using slides — confirm with client).
10. Place timer prominently visible to all teams.
11. Lay out pitch scorecards at the investor seating area.
12. Conduct tech check: laptop, HDMI, PA, timer display.

### Facilitator Briefing Notes

- The brief should be ambitious but achievable — it should create productive tension, not paralysis. Test your brief on a colleague before the event.
- AI brainstorming prompts must be prepared before the event. Recommended prompts: *"What would this solution look like if it were designed for a child?"* / *"What is the most counterintuitive way to solve this problem?"* / *"What would this look like in 50 years?"*
- The facilitator is a coach during the build phase, not a helper. Resist the urge to solve problems for teams. Ask questions: *"What's the core idea here?"* / *"What does the user actually need?"*
- The investor panel should be briefed to ask challenging but fair questions — the goal is to stretch teams, not humiliate them.
- Manage time strictly — the pitching phase tends to run over. Set a hard time limit per pitch (3–4 minutes presentation + 2 minutes investor Q&A).
- Celebrate the process, not just the product — the best pitch doesn't always mean the best team performance.

### Weather/Contingency Considerations

This is an indoor-only experience. Key risks:

- **Materials shortage:** Always bring 20% excess materials (extra tape, cardboard, sticky notes).
- **Team domination by one person:** Introduce a role structure early in the build phase (see Hosting Guide).
- **Brief too hard or too easy:** Have a simpler brief and a harder brief in reserve to adjust on the fly.
- **Investors cancel:** Facilitator plays investor. Brief the most senior client attendee to join the panel.
- **Time runs short:** Cut AI brainstorm phase and give teams a direct brief. Reduce build time to 45 minutes if necessary.

## === SECTION 2: HOSTING GUIDE ===

### Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

> *"Welcome to Creative Collision.*
>
> *I'm going to make you a bold claim right now, and you can hold me to it: by the end of today, your team will have built something that didn't exist this morning. From nothing. Using things that have no business being part of a business solution.*
>
> *You're going to receive a brief. You're going to have a limited amount of time to discuss it, build a prototype, and pitch your solution to a panel of investors — investors who will not be gentle.*
>
> *Here's what I need you to let go of for the next [3/4] hours: the idea that there is a right answer. There isn't. There's your answer — the one your team has the courage to commit to, build, and defend.*
>
> *You don't need experience. You don't need a design degree. You need each other.*
>
> *The winning team won't necessarily have the most polished pitch. They'll have the most interesting idea and the best story about how they got there.*
>
> *Let's begin."*

### Rules Explanation Script

> *"Here's how today works:*
>
> *You have four phases:*
>
> *Phase One — Briefing. You'll receive a brief. You have five minutes to read it as a team and ask me clarifying questions. After five minutes, the brief is the brief.*
>
> *Phase Two — Ideation Sprint. Twenty minutes to generate as many ideas as possible. No judgment. Quantity over quality. I'll give you AI brainstorm prompts to push you into unexpected territory.*
>
> *Phase Three — Build. Sixty minutes to take your best idea and build a physical or illustrated prototype using the materials on your table. Your prototype needs to communicate your idea well enough for an investor to get it.*
>
> *Phase Four — The Pitch. Each team gets [3–4] minutes to pitch and [2] minutes of investor Q&A. Scoring is based on: clarity of the idea, originality, feasibility, and quality of your storytelling.*
>
> *Rules: stay in your workstation area during the build. You may ask me for extra materials — I may or may not have them. You may use your phone during ideation for research only.*
>
> *Any questions? Open your brief envelopes... now."*

### Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

- **Briefing phase (0–25 min):** Energy is curious and cautious. Read the room — some teams will be energised, others overwhelmed. Walk around and observe, not intervene.
- **Ideation sprint (25–45 min):** Energy should be frenetic. Use upbeat music. Drop AI prompts at minutes 5, 12, and 18 to disrupt groupthink. Walk around and celebrate wild ideas.
- **Build phase (45–105 min):** Energy becomes focused. Reduce music volume. Check in with teams at the 30-minute and 50-minute marks. Warn all teams at 10 minutes remaining.
- **Pitch preparation (105–125 min):** Energy shifts to nerves and excitement. Keep energy up verbally.
- **Pitches (125–185 min):** Match the energy of each pitch — if a team is nervous, anchor them with warm encouragement before they start. Celebrate investor questions as a sign of engagement.
- **Awards and debrief (185–210 min):** Celebratory energy. Honour all teams.

### How to Handle Reluctant Participants

- Give reluctant participants a defined role in the build phase: "materials manager," "timekeeper," or "prototype builder" — physical hands-on work often draws reluctant participants in better than discussion.
- In the pitch phase: reluctant speakers can contribute to the pitch preparation without being the presenter.
- Normalise discomfort: *"Creative Collision is supposed to feel uncomfortable — that's how you know you're actually being creative."*

### How to Manage Dominant Participants

- During ideation: *"Let's hear from everyone before we discuss any of these ideas — one idea per person first."*
- During build: assign roles explicitly if one person is directing everything: *"You're the builder, you're the communicator, you're the client perspective-holder."*
- During pitching: if one person has taken over the pitch, privately suggest before they go on stage that the team considers a two- or three-person delivery.

### Common Problems and How to Solve Them

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Teams don't understand the brief | Walk each table and ask *"What do you think the brief is asking?"* — paraphrase back until aligned |
| Teams get stuck in ideation and have no concept by close | Prompt them: *"Which idea on your sheet is most interesting to you? Pick that one."* |
| Build runs over time | Call time firmly — incomplete prototypes pitch as-is; the facilitator celebrates the concept regardless |
| A pitch falls flat | Investor and facilitator respond to the courage, not the quality: *"I love the ambition here — tell me more about..."* |
| Investors are too harsh | Brief investors before the event on psychological safety — if they cross a line, interject: *"That's a strong challenge — let the team respond"* |
| Two teams have similar concepts | Celebrate it: *"Great minds. Now let's see whose execution wins."* |

### Safety Considerations

- Craft knives should be kept at the facilitator station and issued on request only — not left at tables.
- Ensure adequate ventilation if glue guns are used (confirm with client before adding to materials kit).
- Electrical items or candles are not to be used in the build phase.
- Confirm first aid contact at venue.

## === SECTION 3: DEBRIEF GUIDE ===

### Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

Creative Collision generates enormous energy and often produces surprising results — unexpected team members shine, unconventional ideas win, and comfortable thinkers are challenged. Without a debrief, these insights dissipate within 24 hours. The debrief converts the emotional experience of creating under pressure into transferable insight about innovation, collaboration, and risk-taking.

### Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

- Debrief after the awards.
- Duration: **25–30 minutes**
- Format: Whole group, with a brief pair reflection moment in the middle.

### 10 Debrief Questions (From Surface to Deep)

1. *"What was the first idea your team came up with — and why didn't you go with it?"*
2. *"When did your team's concept actually click into place? What happened in that moment?"*
3. *"Who on your team surprised you today — and what did they do?"*
4. *"What would you have done differently in the ideation sprint if you had 10 more minutes?"*
5. *"How did your team make the decision to commit to one idea? Was it agreed, voted, or led by someone?"*
6. *"Where did the AI prompts help, confuse, or unlock something unexpected?"*
7. *"When did you feel most out of your comfort zone — and what did you do with that feeling?"*
8. *"What does the way your team built and pitched today tell you about how you approach new challenges at work?"*
9. *"What conditions does your team need to do its best creative work? Are those conditions present in your day-to-day environment?"*
10. *"If your team had a 'Creative Collision' moment in your actual work — an open brief, unusual constraints, 60 minutes — what would you build?"*

### How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

- The brief mirrors every real-world project kick-off — ambiguity, pressure, and the need to commit before you have full information.
- The ideation sprint surfaces the innovation culture of the team: do they build on each other's ideas, or compete to have the right idea?
- The pitch experience is transferable to every internal presentation, proposal, or stakeholder conversation — storytelling under scrutiny is a learnable skill.
- The investor panel replicates the experience of defending a budget, a strategy, or a recommendation in front of senior leadership.

### How to Close the Debrief Powerfully

> *"What you did today is exactly what the best teams in the world do when they're faced with a problem that has no obvious solution: they showed up, they disagreed productively, they made something, and they stood behind it.*
>
> *That is not a creative skill. That is a courage skill. And you already have it.*
>
> *The question is: where in your work are you not using it? Where are you waiting for more information, more certainty, more permission — when actually what you need is to start building?*
>
> *Take one idea from today — not a prototype. A behaviour. Something you'll try differently. Write it down.*
>
> *That is your Creative Collision takeaway."*

### What to Leave the Group With

- A named behavioural commitment
- Recognition of specific team members who demonstrated unexpected strengths
- A prototype they built together — physical artefact of the experience (teams keep their prototypes)
- Awareness of what conditions enable or block their team's best creative work

#### Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to be creative to enjoy this?**
The experience is designed to unlock creativity in people who don't consider themselves creative. The constraints, the materials, and the time pressure work together to produce ideas you wouldn't generate in a normal meeting.

**What kind of problem will we be solving?**
The brief is a real-world challenge — either drawn from your industry or designed specifically for your team. It will be ambitious enough to challenge you and open enough to allow multiple valid solutions.

**Will I have to present?**
Your team pitches together — who presents is your team's decision. You can nominate a speaker, split the pitch, or rotate. The investor panel scores the pitch, not the presenter.

**What if our idea is bad?**
There are no bad ideas in this experience. The investors and facilitator are looking for originality and commitment — a bold, unusual solution pitched well will outscore a safe, obvious solution every time.
