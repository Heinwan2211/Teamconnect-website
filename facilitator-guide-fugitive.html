Facilitator Guide: The Fugitive

High-Intensity Urban Pursuit Experience | Cape Town Urban and Outdoor | 20–500 Participants | 2–4 Hours | Outdoor

SECTION 1: FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE

Experience Overview and Objectives

The Fugitive is a high-intensity urban pursuit experience set in Cape Town's urban and outdoor environments. Teams are divided into hunters and fugitives. Fugitives are given a head start and must navigate the city using encrypted clues to reach safety points. Hunters receive clue sequences to intercept and capture fugitives before they complete their route. A rapidly shrinking time window creates constant pressure — the longer the experience runs, the harder it becomes to survive. This is Team Connect's most physically demanding and logistically complex experience, requiring thorough venue mapping, meticulous clue placement, and robust safety protocols.

Primary objectives:

  • Build decision-making skills under time pressure and uncertainty
  • Develop strategic thinking and adaptive planning
  • Strengthen communication and coordination within and across mobile teams
  • Create high-adrenaline shared experiences that generate lasting team memories
  • Surface leadership, initiative, and composure under pressure

Ideal Group Size and Duration

  • Minimum: 20 participants (5 teams of 4 — minimum viable for hunter/fugitive dynamic)
  • Maximum: 500 participants (with full facilitation team and zone management)
  • Optimal group size: 50–200 participants
  • Team size: 4–6 people per team
  • Role split: Approximately 40% fugitives, 60% hunters (or equal — confirm with client based on experience design)
  • Total duration: 2–4 hours
    • Briefing and team assignment: 20–30 minutes
    • Active pursuit phase: 90–150 minutes (scaling with participant count and zone complexity)
    • Return and debrief: 30–40 minutes

Venue Mapping

Venue mapping is the most critical preparation task for The Fugitive. A poorly mapped zone creates safety failures, navigation confusion, and participant disengagement.

Mapping steps:

  1. Pre-event reconnaissance (minimum 72 hours before):
    • Walk the entire zone on foot. Do not rely on maps alone.
    • Identify all entry and exit points, road crossings, and areas requiring additional safety management.
    • Note areas to avoid: construction zones, high-traffic roads, private property, restricted access areas.
    • Identify landmark reference points that appear clearly in clues.
    • Test navigation instructions at key waypoints — can a person unfamiliar with the area follow the clue to the correct location?
  2. Zone boundary definition:
    • Define the play zone precisely with GPS coordinates and/or clear landmark boundaries.
    • Boundaries must be communicated clearly to all participants before the game begins.
    • Out-of-bounds areas must be named explicitly: "The play zone does not extend beyond [STREET NAME] to the north or [STREET NAME] to the east."
  3. Checkpoint and safe-point placement:
    • Identify 5–8 checkpoints per route (scale with total duration).
    • Safe points must be publicly accessible locations — do not use private property without written permission.
    • Confirm all checkpoint locations are accessible to participants with varying physical fitness levels.
    • Each checkpoint must be staffable by a Team Connect marshal during the event.
  4. Route design:
    • Fugitive routes and hunter interception points must not create dangerous convergence at road crossings or high-traffic areas.
    • Design routes to include both open areas and narrower urban passages — variety maintains engagement.
    • Confirm route can be completed within the allotted time window at a brisk walking pace.

Clue Placement

Clues must be precise, weatherproof, and positioned exactly as tested during reconnaissance.

Clue design principles:

  • Each clue must be independently solvable without prior knowledge of Cape Town.
  • Clues should include a navigation instruction AND a verification element (something participants confirm at the location).
  • Use encrypted formats: cipher-based clues, coordinate fragments, or image-based directional clues.
  • Clue difficulty should escalate as the time window shrinks — early clues are easier to allow teams to get into the flow.

Clue placement procedure:

  • Place clues minimum 60 minutes before start time.
  • Use weatherproof containers or clue sleeves at outdoor locations.
  • Record GPS coordinates and photos of each clue placement — bring this record to the event.
  • Assign a clue recovery team to collect all clue placements after the event concludes.

Digital clue system (if used):

  • Confirm platform functions on participant devices before clue placement.
  • Test clue delivery at each checkpoint location — confirm no dead zones in mobile coverage.
  • Maintain offline backup of all clues in case of platform failure.

Safety Protocols

The Fugitive involves participants moving through active urban environments. Safety is the primary facilitator responsibility.

Pre-event safety protocols:

  • Conduct full risk assessment of the venue zone: hazards, traffic, crowd density, accessibility.
  • Assign safety marshals: minimum 1 marshal per 50 participants, plus dedicated safety lead.
  • Identify first aid stations: one central location at base, and nearest public first aid point in the zone.
  • Establish emergency communication protocol: all participants carry emergency contact card with event number.
  • Confirm all participants have been briefed on safety rules before the game begins.
  • Identify the nearest medical facility to the play zone.

During-event safety protocols:

  • All marshals carry mobile phones and are in continuous contact with the safety lead.
  • Establish a clear abort signal: a specific SMS, siren code, or platform notification that immediately ends the game and directs all participants to the base location.
  • Teams must check in at each checkpoint — missed check-ins trigger a welfare check within 5 minutes.
  • No participant may enter restricted, private, or out-of-bounds areas. Disqualification is immediate.
  • If a participant is injured: safety lead contacts emergency services; marshal stays with injured participant; game continues unless safety lead determines wider abort is required.

Safety rules communicated to all participants before start:

  • Stay within the play zone at all times.
  • Do not enter private property.
  • Obey all traffic rules — road crossings are at pedestrian crossings only.
  • No running in restricted areas (certain urban zones, crowded areas, or areas designated in the briefing).
  • If you are unsure whether an area is in bounds, it is out of bounds.
  • Emergency number: [INSERT EVENT EMERGENCY NUMBER].

Managing Large Outdoor Groups

For events above 100 participants, additional management structures are required:

  • Zone management: Divide the play zone into sub-zones, each with a dedicated marshal.
  • Wave starts: Stagger fugitive releases in waves of 10–15 minutes to prevent crowd convergence at early checkpoints.
  • Communication infrastructure: Two-way radio for marshal team is mandatory above 100 participants.
  • Central operations point: Designate a central base where safety lead, scoring, and client sponsor are positioned throughout the event.
  • Contingency headcount: All participants must be accounted for at the conclusion of the game before the debrief begins.

Venue and Space Requirements

  • Play zone: Cape Town urban and outdoor environments (city centre, waterfront, Green Point, Gardens, Signal Hill — confirm with client based on experience design)
  • Base location: A fixed, identifiable central point participants return to after the game
  • Briefing space: Indoor or covered outdoor space for pre-game briefing (minimum 1 m² per participant)

Equipment and Materials Checklist

Logistics and navigation:

  • Clue sets: printed, laminated, placed at all checkpoints
  • Zone maps (printed — one per team, plus additional for marshals)
  • Team identity materials: coloured bibs or wristbands (hunter vs. fugitive designation)
  • Checkpoint materials: marker flags, checkpoint stamps, or digital check-in tokens

Communications:

  • Two-way radios: minimum one per marshal for events over 100 participants
  • Mobile phones: all marshals with shared contact list
  • Emergency contact cards: one per participant

Safety:

  • First aid kit: at base and with safety lead
  • Emergency contact list: venue, medical facility, client sponsor
  • Incident report forms (printed)

AV for briefing:

  • Wireless microphone and portable PA (for outdoor briefing)
  • Printed briefing packs per team (backup to verbal brief)

Facilitator Briefing Notes

  • Know the entire route before the event. Every facilitator and marshal must have walked the zone.
  • The excitement of the experience can cause participants to push boundaries — safety rules must be communicated firmly and enforced consistently.
  • Manage the time window actively: if teams are ahead of schedule, compress; if behind, extend checkpoints or reduce the total number of clues.
  • The hunter/fugitive split is emotionally charged — assign roles deliberately or allow teams to self-assign, but confirm the split in advance.
  • For large events, you are a zone manager, not just a host. Know where every team is at all times through your marshal network.

Weather/Contingency Considerations

  • Rain: The Fugitive is rain-affected but not rain-cancelled. Light rain is manageable; heavy rain or thunder/lightning requires abort. Confirm wet weather decision point with client minimum 3 hours before start.
  • Extreme heat: Carry additional water for all participants. Consider starting early morning. Abort if heat index is dangerous.
  • Crowd or event disruption in the city: Know the city's event calendar for the day. Avoid zones where other events are occurring.
  • Participant injury: Follow safety protocol — see above.
  • Platform failure: Revert to printed clue backup. Marshall delivers next clue to team on radio instruction.

Pre-Event Client Communication Checklist

  • Venue zone confirmed and reconnoitred
  • Safety risk assessment completed
  • Marshal team assigned and briefed
  • Clue sets designed, printed, and placed
  • Participant emergency contact cards prepared
  • Zone maps printed — per team
  • Client briefed on safety protocols and contingency plan
  • Participant information sheet distributed (Section 5)
  • Headcount confirmed — team assignments prepared
  • Wet weather decision point agreed with client

SECTION 2: HOSTING GUIDE

Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

"Good [MORNING / AFTERNOON], everyone. Welcome to The Fugitive.

In about [X] minutes, [NUMBER] of you are going to become fugitives. The rest of you are going to hunt them down.

Here is the situation. The fugitives have been given a route. They have encrypted clues leading them to safety points across [ZONE NAME]. Their mission: reach the final safe point before the time runs out.

The hunters have a different set of clues. Their clues lead to interception points — locations where the fugitives' route converges. Capture a fugitive by tagging them, and they're done.

Here's the pressure: the time window is shrinking. Right now, you have [X] minutes. In [X] minutes, you'll have less. The longer this takes, the harder it gets.

This is not a race. It is a pursuit. Strategy beats speed. Communication beats individual heroics. The team that thinks best under pressure wins.

Before we start — safety. This is the most important part of this briefing. [DELIVER SAFETY BRIEF — see below]. Any questions on safety? [Pause and address any questions.]

Now — team assignments are on your brief cards. Fugitives — you leave in [X] minutes. Hunters — you follow [X] minutes after. Good luck. You're going to need it."

Safety Brief Script (Mandatory — Delivered Word for Word)

"Before we go anywhere — safety rules. These are not suggestions. These are the rules.

One: the play zone is [DESCRIBE BOUNDARIES EXPLICITLY]. Do not go beyond these boundaries. If you are unsure whether you are in bounds, you are out of bounds.

Two: no private property. If it has a fence, a door, or a sign saying keep out — it is out of bounds. Full stop.

Three: road crossings at pedestrian crossings only. Do not run across roads.

Four: no running in [SPECIFY ANY RESTRICTED RUNNING AREAS — crowded public spaces, certain streets].

Five: check in at every checkpoint. Missed check-in triggers a welfare call within five minutes.

Six: emergency number is [INSERT NUMBER]. Put it in your phone now. [Pause.] If there is an emergency, call this number. One of our team will respond immediately.

Seven: if you hear [ABORT SIGNAL — e.g., three short blasts on the siren / a specific SMS], stop immediately and return to [BASE LOCATION].

Any questions on safety? [Pause.] Good. You're ready."

Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

  • Briefing: High energy, urgent. Build tension before releasing the teams — the launch moment should feel dramatic.
  • During the game: Facilitator is mobile and monitoring, not entertaining. Energy comes from the game itself.
  • At checkpoints: Keep check-in moments brief and tense. "You've got [X] minutes left. Your next clue is inside. Go."
  • Game end / return to base: Reset from high-adrenaline to calm. Allow teams to arrive and recover before debrief.
  • Debrief: Lower energy, reflective, but still emotionally charged from the experience.

How to Handle Reluctant Participants

  • Reluctance in an outdoor pursuit is often about physical confidence.
  • Assign reluctant participants to tactical roles that do not require physical speed: navigator, code-breaker, communications lead.
  • Frame the experience around strategy, not fitness: "The fastest team doesn't always win. The smartest one does."

How to Manage Dominant Participants

  • Dominant participants in pursuit games often try to sprint and split from their team.
  • Brief teams before start: "You win or lose as a team. A solo operator who covers ground without their team loses the clue-solving advantage."
  • If a team member repeatedly ignores team decisions: address with team captain privately at a checkpoint.

SECTION 3: DEBRIEF GUIDE

Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

The Fugitive generates intense, genuine experience: real decisions under real pressure, real consequences for poor communication, and real moments of strategic success or failure. The debrief is the space where that intensity is converted into insight. Without it, participants leave with adrenaline and stories. With it, they leave with self-knowledge and transferable skills.

Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

  • Allow 10–15 minutes after the game for teams to gather and settle before the debrief begins.
  • Duration: 30–40 minutes
  • Format: Whole group for opening questions; small team reflection for depth; whole group for synthesis.

10 Debrief Questions (From Surface to Deep)

  1. "What happened? Tell me the story of your team's game — from the moment you started to the moment you finished."
  2. "What was the first decision your team made, and who made it? Was that the right decision?"
  3. "When did the pressure hit hardest? What did your team do in that moment?"
  4. "What would you do differently if you ran this again with the same team?"
  5. "Who in your team did something today that surprised you — positively or negatively?"
  6. "When was communication your greatest advantage? When did it break down?"
  7. "What was the role of strategy in your team — did you have one, or were you reacting?"
  8. "What happened in your team when things went wrong — did you freeze, adapt, or blame each other?"
  9. "Where in your actual work does your team face the same pressure — shrinking time, incomplete information, the need to move fast without slowing down to think?"
  10. "What is one thing about how your team performed today that you want to carry back into your work?"

How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

  • The shrinking time window mirrors project deadlines and crisis management — teams that communicated well under pressure had a system, not just luck.
  • Hunter and fugitive roles surface different team profiles: the hunters who planned vs. the hunters who sprinted; the fugitives who stayed calm vs. those who panicked.
  • Missed check-ins are a workplace metaphor: the team member who goes dark under pressure, the colleague who operates solo without updating the team.
  • Clue decryption under time pressure mirrors information processing in complex, fast-moving work environments.

How to Close the Debrief Powerfully

"You've just spent [X] hours in a city, under pressure, with incomplete information, trying to outthink and outperform people who were trying to stop you.

That is not a game. That is a training ground.

The decisions you made, the way you communicated, the moments you led and the moments you defaulted — all of that is real. It happens at work. It happens in every high-pressure moment your team faces.

[Pause]

What do you want to do differently? You've had a good look at how your team works under pressure today. Don't waste it.

Take 60 seconds. Write down one thing. Not a vague intention — a specific behaviour. What will you do differently the next time the pressure is on and the time window is shrinking?"

What to Leave the Group With

  • A written, specific behavioural commitment
  • Named moments of genuine team performance to reference going forward
  • The language for their team's key dynamic under pressure

SECTION 5: CLIENT INFORMATION PACKAGE

This section is a ready-to-send document for clients to share with their participants.

The Fugitive — What You Need to Know

Your team experience is coming up — here's everything you need to know before the day.

About the Experience

The Fugitive is a high-intensity urban pursuit experience set across Cape Town's streets and outdoor spaces. Teams are split into hunters and fugitives. Fugitives use encrypted clues to navigate to safety. Hunters use interception clues to stop them. A shrinking time window means the pressure never stops.

This is team building with real stakes, real decisions, and real adrenaline.

Duration2–4 hours
FormatTeams of 4–6 competing across a defined urban zone
VenueCape Town urban and outdoor environment (zone details provided on the day)

What to Expect on the Day

  • Arrive at the base location and register with your facilitator.
  • Receive your team assignment: hunter or fugitive.
  • Receive your clue pack and play zone map.
  • Your facilitator will brief you on the rules and safety requirements.
  • Fugitives launch first. Hunters follow.
  • Navigate your route, solve clues, and either evade or intercept — depending on your role.
  • Return to base when the game ends (time limit) or your team is captured / reaches safety.
  • Join the group debrief and results ceremony.

What to Wear and Bring

  • Footwear: Closed-toe, comfortable walking shoes. No sandals or heels. You may be walking several kilometres.
  • Clothing: Comfortable, weather-appropriate active wear. Layers recommended — Cape Town weather changes.
  • What to bring: Fully charged mobile phone. Water bottle. Sunscreen (outdoor event). Energy snack.
  • What to leave behind: Bags are a liability. If you must carry something, use a small backpack.

Important Safety Information

  • The experience takes place in active urban environments.
  • You will receive a full safety brief before the game begins — pay attention.
  • An emergency contact number will be provided. Save it in your phone before you leave the base.
  • Stay within the play zone at all times. Zone boundaries will be clearly communicated.
  • Road crossings at pedestrian crossings only.
  • If you are injured or need help, call the emergency number immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to be fit?
The Fugitive involves walking, not running. The emphasis is on strategy and navigation, not physical speed. Participants with mobility limitations should inform their event coordinator in advance — adjustments can be made to route and role.

What if the weather is bad?
The event runs in light rain. Heavy rain or extreme weather triggers a contingency plan. Your event coordinator will communicate any weather-related changes by [INSERT TIME — e.g., 7am on the day].

What if I get lost?
Your team will have a zone map and emergency contact card. Marshals are positioned throughout the zone. If you are unsure of your location, call the emergency number.

Can I use Google Maps?
You may use mapping apps for orientation, but clue solutions must be solved by your team — not looked up online. Marshals may disqualify teams observed using search engines for clue solutions.
