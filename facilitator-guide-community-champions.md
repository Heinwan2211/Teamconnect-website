# Facilitator Guide: Community Champions
### CSR Team Building Experience | Impact Scoring | Handover Ceremony | 3–4 Hours | Any Venue

---

## === SECTION 1: FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE ===

### Experience Overview and Objectives

Community Champions is a CSR-focused team building experience where teams design and execute a micro community project. Teams choose from a selection of impact tracks — assembling food garden kits for schools, building habitat structures for wildlife, or preparing care packages for shelters — and compete on a real-time impact scoring system that measures quality, teamwork, and creativity. The experience closes with a handover ceremony where completed projects are formally presented to representatives from beneficiary organisations.

**Primary objectives:**
- Create genuine shared purpose by connecting team effort to real community impact
- Build collaboration through meaningful, hands-on work with visible outcomes
- Support company ESG and CSR commitments with a documentable impact event
- Bridge the gap between competitive team dynamics and collective contribution
- Generate lasting team memory through an emotionally resonant shared experience

### Ideal Group Size and Duration

- **Minimum:** 10 participants (2 teams of 5)
- **Maximum:** 300 participants (with multiple impact tracks and simultaneous teams)
- **Optimal group size:** 30–150 participants
- **Team size:** 5–10 people per team
- **Total duration:** 3–4 hours
  - Welcome and brief: 20 minutes
  - Impact track work: 120–150 minutes
  - Impact scoring and celebration: 20 minutes
  - Handover ceremony: 20–30 minutes
  - Debrief: 20 minutes

### Venue and Space Requirements

- **Any indoor or outdoor venue** — flexible
- Each team needs a clear workspace for their impact track (approximately 6m x 3m per team)
- Tables for assembly work (one long table per team)
- If food garden kits: flat surface for soil, pots, seedlings — allow for mess and water access nearby
- If habitat structures: floor space for construction; confirm any restrictions on tools with venue
- If care packages: large tables for sorting, packing, and quality-checking
- Shared zone for handover ceremony — where beneficiary representatives can be received
- Outdoor option: parking area, grass area, or courtyard works well for garden kit and habitat tracks
- Adequate shade if outdoor in summer

### Equipment and Materials Checklist

**Team Connect coordinates and provides:**
- Impact track materials per chosen activity:
  - *Food garden kits:* seedling trays, soil/potting mix, pots/containers, plant labels, gardening gloves, watering cans, instruction cards for beneficiaries
  - *Habitat structures:* untreated wood offcuts, screws, sandpaper, non-toxic paint, brushes, measurement tools, assembly guides, pre-drilled panels (optional)
  - *Care packages:* sorted donation items (toiletries, non-perishable food, warm clothing, activity booklets), packaging bags or boxes, ribbon, labels, personalised message cards
- Impact scoring scoreboards (per team, tracked visually)
- Scoring criteria cards (per team): quality, team contribution, creativity, efficiency
- Facilitator scoring paddles or tablets
- Timer display
- PA system and wireless microphone
- Camera or device for impact documentation (photos)
- Handover ceremony backdrop/display (Team Connect or client branded)
- Beneficiary representative invitation (facilitated by Team Connect in coordination with client)
- Prize certificates (for winning team)

**Client/venue provides:**
- Tables and workspaces
- Water access (for garden tracks)
- Waste / cleaning materials

### Setup Instructions (Step by Step)

1. Arrive 90 minutes before participants.
2. Set up team workstations — one per team, clearly separated, materials laid out but not yet fully accessible.
3. Set up scoring display — visible to all teams.
4. Set up handover ceremony area: draped table or display zone for completed projects, space for beneficiary representatives to stand and receive.
5. Test PA system and microphone from every zone.
6. Lay out impact scoring criteria cards at each workstation.
7. Confirm beneficiary representative's arrival time — they should arrive 20 minutes before the handover ceremony.
8. Set up impact documentation station — camera or phone on tripod, or assign a team photographer.
9. Prepare scoring sheets and submit to co-facilitator/scorekeepers.
10. Lay out team name markers at each workstation.
11. Arrange prizes at facilitator station.
12. Final check: all materials accounted for per team, nothing missing from kits.

### Facilitator Briefing Notes

- Community Champions is the most emotionally resonant experience in the portfolio — the facilitator's role is to hold space for both the competitive and the meaningful.
- Coordinate the beneficiary organisation well in advance — confirm their attendance for the handover ceremony at least two weeks prior. A representative's presence at the close transforms the experience.
- Impact scoring should be conducted by a roving facilitator or co-facilitator who scores teams at regular intervals — not by the MC alone. Establish clear scoring intervals (every 20 minutes).
- Know the beneficiary organisation's story. Reference it specifically during the welcome and handover — the more personal the connection, the more meaningful the work.
- Celebrate process as much as output: teams that work joyfully, inclusively, and creatively should be rewarded even if their physical output is not the largest.
- Photography is key — document the event for the client's CSR reporting. Assign a dedicated photographer or designate a team member per team.

### Weather/Contingency Considerations

- **Rain (outdoor event):** Have an indoor backup plan identified before the event. Garden kit track can be moved indoors if surfaces allow for moisture.
- **Beneficiary representative cancels:** Arrange for a video message from the organisation to be played at the ceremony as backup. Client representative can step in to receive the handover symbolically.
- **Materials shortage:** Always bring 10–15% excess materials per track. For garden kits, extra seedlings and soil are essential (some will be damaged in transport).
- **Teams finish early:** Have a "bonus impact task" prepared — personalised message cards, decoration of packaging, or additional items to build.

## === SECTION 2: HOSTING GUIDE ===

### Welcome and Opening Script (Word for Word)

> *"Welcome, everyone — welcome to Community Champions.*
>
> *In a moment, I'm going to ask your team to build something. Not a prototype, not a pitch deck, not a strategy document. Something real. Something that will outlast today.*
>
> *[Pause]*
>
> *Every kit on your table, every material in your workstation — it's going somewhere after this experience. It's going to [name the beneficiary organisation]. It's going to [name of school / shelter / wildlife project]. And the people who receive it... they're going to use it.*
>
> *Today you're competing — yes. There's a scoring system, there are points, there will be a winner. But I want you to hold two things at once today: compete fiercely, and build with care. Because the score reflects the quality of what your community receives.*
>
> *This is team building with a purpose. Let's make it count."*

### Rules Explanation Script

> *"Here's how the scoring works:*
>
> *Your team earns points in four categories:*
>
> *One — Quality. How well-made is your final product? Will it actually serve its purpose for the person who receives it?*
>
> *Two — Team Contribution. Are all team members actively contributing? Our facilitators are watching — silent passengers don't earn full points.*
>
> *Three — Creativity. Did your team go beyond the basics? Did you personalise, decorate, or add unexpected value?*
>
> *Four — Efficiency. Did you manage your time and materials well? Less waste, more output.*
>
> *Scoring happens every [20] minutes. The leaderboard updates after each scoring interval.*
>
> *The winning team is announced before the handover ceremony — and they earn the honour of leading the handover on behalf of everyone.*
>
> *Any questions? Then let's begin."*

### Energy Management Tips Throughout the Experience

- **Opening (0–20 min):** Energy is engaged and purposeful. Keep it warm — this is not a high-adrenaline launch, it's a meaningful one.
- **Early work phase (20–60 min):** Teams settle into their tasks. Move between teams and ask curious questions: *"What are you making here? Who's this for?"* Keep the emotional frame of the beneficiary alive throughout.
- **Mid-phase (60–90 min):** Energy can dip as repetitive assembly tasks become routine. Do a mid-point "impact snapshot" — total up what's been made across all teams and announce it aloud. *"As of right now, we've assembled X food garden kits. That feeds X families."*
- **Final phase (90–120 min):** Increase energy as teams approach completion. Celebrate visible quality and creativity aloud.
- **Handover ceremony (120–150 min):** Shift to ceremonial, reflective energy. Music softens. MC slows down. Space is held for emotion — it is appropriate for participants to feel moved.

### How to Handle Reluctant Participants

- Physical, hands-on tasks are naturally inclusive — most reluctant participants become engaged when given a clear, specific task: *"Can you assemble this section?"*
- If someone is still disengaged, position them as the "quality checker" — reviewing completed items rather than building from scratch.
- Never force participation or draw attention to someone who is staying back. Physical community work tends to draw people in naturally.

### How to Manage Dominant Participants

- In assembly tasks, role distribution happens naturally — one person can't do everything at once.
- If one person is directing all decisions: introduce an explicit role rotation: *"For the next 20 minutes, let's have someone else lead the quality check."*
- Channel dominant energy into documentation: *"Could you be in charge of capturing photos and telling the story of what your team is building?"*

### Common Problems and How to Solve Them

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Teams run out of materials | Draw from facilitator reserve stock — never let a team sit idle |
| Beneficiary rep is late | Fill with additional debrief time; rep joins the close if they arrive late |
| Teams finish the impact task early | Deploy bonus personalisation task: write message cards, decorate packaging |
| Assembly task too complex for some teams | Facilitator steps in briefly to demonstrate technique, then steps back |
| Scoring dispute | Reinforce that facilitator decisions are final; acknowledge good faith effort |
| One team's output is significantly lower quality | Before ceremony, facilitator quietly assists that team to bring quality up to standard — never shame them |

### Safety Considerations

- Garden tools (trowels, scissors for seedling packs): keep in facilitator care and distribute responsibly.
- Woodwork track: sandpaper, non-sharp tools only. No power tools unless client explicitly requests and venue allows.
- Ensure no participants have severe allergies to soil, plants, or packaging materials — confirm with client in advance.
- Outdoor venues: sunscreen reminder, shade, water for participants.
- Confirm first aid contact at venue.

## === SECTION 3: DEBRIEF GUIDE ===

### Why Debrief Matters for This Experience

Community Champions is inherently meaningful — but the meaning can remain implicit without a debrief. Participants feel good after the experience but may not connect it to their workplace behaviour or team culture. The debrief converts the emotional high of the handover ceremony into insight about purpose, contribution, and how teams create value together.

### Debrief Timing (When and How Long)

- Debrief after the handover ceremony — let the emotional impact of the ceremony settle first.
- Duration: **20–25 minutes**
- Format: Whole group, with a brief individual reflection moment.

### 10 Debrief Questions (From Surface to Deep)

1. *"What's one thing your team made today — and who will use it?"*
2. *"At what point during the work did you feel most connected to your team?"*
3. *"Was there a moment today when the purpose of the work changed how you approached it? What happened?"*
4. *"What role did you naturally play today — builder, coordinator, quality checker, encourager? Is that the role you usually play at work?"*
5. *"When did your team have to make a decision together? How did you make it?"*
6. *"How did the scoring system change how you worked? Did competition make you better or distract you from the purpose?"*
7. *"What would it look like if your team brought this same focus and care to your work every day?"*
8. *"What does your team do at work that has a community impact — even if it's not as visible as today?"*
9. *"When [beneficiary representative name] spoke today, what landed for you? What will you remember?"*
10. *"If your team could choose one thing to give to the communities around you in the next six months, what would it be — and what would it take to make it happen?"*

### How to Connect the Experience to Workplace Behaviour

- The scoring system mirrors every KPI, performance metric, and project deadline — it shapes behaviour. The debrief asks: does your current measurement system bring out the best in your team?
- Quality, creativity, efficiency, and contribution are the same criteria by which great teams measure their work in every context. Naming this connection is powerful.
- The handover ceremony shows teams that their work has real recipients — a mirror for every deliverable, report, and service their company produces.
- Teams that maintained purpose under competitive pressure demonstrated a skill that is rare and valuable in any work environment.

### How to Close the Debrief Powerfully

> *"Today you didn't just build [describe what was built]. You chose to show up — to give your time and your effort to something beyond yourselves. And you did it together.*
>
> *That's the thing about purpose: it doesn't make hard work easier. It makes it worthwhile.*
>
> *Here's what I want you to take back with you: your team already knows how to work with purpose. You proved that today. The question is — how do you bring that into Monday morning?*
>
> *What's the purpose behind the work your team does every day? Do your people know it? Do they feel it?*
>
> *One thing. One specific way you'll make that purpose more visible for your team. Write it down.*
>
> *That's your Community Champions commitment."*

### What to Leave the Group With

- A named commitment to making purpose more visible in their day-to-day work
- A photo or physical reminder of what they built and who it serves (provide group photos if possible)
- The name of their beneficiary organisation and the specific impact made today
- A shared emotional memory of the handover ceremony

#### Frequently Asked Questions

**What kind of project will we be working on?**
Your event coordinator has selected the impact track for your group. You'll be informed on the day, but expect hands-on assembly or construction work that directly benefits a community organisation.

**Who receives what we build?**

All tracks have roles that don't require heavy physical activity — quality inspection, coordination, labelling, personalisation. No one is excluded. Let your team coordinator know in advance if you have physical limitations.
